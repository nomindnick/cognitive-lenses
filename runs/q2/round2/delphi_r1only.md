# Facilitator's Report: Persistent Disagreement After Final Round

## Overview

Numerically, the panel converged tightly (68–72, median 70, IQR collapsed at 70). The persistent disagreement is therefore almost entirely **qualitative** — it lives in the panelists' stated uncertainties and in divergent reasoning paths to similar numbers. Four distinct fault lines remain.

---## 1. The status of the rate study's "in part" admission

**Crux on one side:** Several panelists treat the study's language — that $0.85 was derived "in part" from conservation-incentive pricing — as a near-dispositive, self-inflicted admission of the precise defect condemned in *Capistrano*, especially given that the agency bears the burden of proof under section 6(b).

**Crux on the other side:** Other panelists (most explicitly the one whose stated uncertainty turns on this) hold open that a court could read the same language as "an additional policy rationale layered atop an otherwise-adequate 1.6x cost-of-service study" — i.e., rhetorical framing rather than proof the figure was not cost-derived.

**Source of split:** A mix of (i) a **factual gap** — the hypothetical omits the base-tier rate and the dollar decomposition of $0.85, so no panelist could test whether the surcharge exceeds the documented 1.6x differential — and (ii) a **genuinely unsettled legal question** about how literally courts will read mixed-motive language in rate studies.

**What would resolve it:** The rate-study workpapers (base-tier rate; how much of $0.85 corresponds to the 1.6x differential versus an incentive markup). Four panelists independently identified this as their biggest uncertainty, making it the panel's dominant unresolved factual variable.

---

## 2. Severability and remedy — what counts as "substantial part"

**Crux on one side:** Panelists at the higher end reason that partial remedies (severing the incentive increment, striking the rebate-funded share, or even court-ordered recalculation) satisfy the proposition's "in whole or in substantial part" framing, which raises the probability regardless of how the merits shake out.

**Crux on the other side:** The panelist at 68 gives more weight to the possibility that a court, crediting the genuine 1.6x differential and the conservation mandate, upholds the surcharge "largely intact" — treating the defects as non-severable from a reasonable overall methodology.

**Source of split:** Primarily **differing weighting of the district's defenses** (a mild risk-tolerance difference), layered over an **unsettled legal question**: whether post-*Capistrano* courts strike defective components or order reform/recalculation, and whether the latter counts as invalidation "in substantial part."

**What would resolve it:** A court's actual remedial order in this or an analogous case; or appellate clarification of severability practice for blended rate structures.

---

## 3. Whether the rebate spending can be recharacterized as cost of service

**Crux on one side:** Most panelists treat the district's concession — that rebates "mostly benefit customers other than those paying" — as a largely self-proving cross-subsidy violation under 6(b)(2)/(3), independent of the pricing theory.

**Crux on the other side:** Two panelists flag as unresolved whether the district could recharacterize rebate and education spending as **system-wide demand reduction** that lowers peaking/drought-reserve costs attributable to the paying tier — an avoided-cost theory that would convert the apparent cross-subsidy into cost recovery.

**Source of split:** A **genuinely unsettled legal question** (whether avoided-cost benefits satisfy parcel-specific proportionality) combined with a **factual assumption** about whether quantifiable avoided-cost evidence exists in the record.

**What would resolve it:** Evidence quantifying system-wide cost savings from the rebate program and attributing them to high-tier service costs; or precedent directly addressing demand-reduction spending as a cost of service.
