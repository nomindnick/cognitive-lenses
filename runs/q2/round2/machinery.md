## I. Rate-Adoption Record (Art. XIII D § 6(a) track — already occurred, but its integrity feeds every downstream claim/defense)

- Cal. Const. art. XIII D, § 6(a)(1) — mailed notice ≥45 days before hearing, triggered by board's intent to adopt/increase a property-related fee — **confirm** actual mailing date vs. hearing date, and that notice went to "each identified parcel" per Gov. Code § 53755.
- Gov. Code § 53755 — prescribes required notice content (amount, basis, protest procedure) — **confirm** notice disclosed the "conservation-incentive" component vs. pure cost allocation, since inadequate disclosure is an independent § 6(a) procedural defect separate from the § 6(b) substantive claim.
- Cal. Const. art. XIII D, § 6(a)(2) — majority-protest procedure; fee fails only if protests from a majority of affected parcels — 9% protest here is far below threshold — **confirm** the denominator used ("parcels" vs. some other unit) and that only record owners/ratepayers of the parcel class actually charged were counted (relevant if a later claim alleges protest-tabulation error).
- Cal. Const. art. XIII D, § 6(c) — water, sewer, and refuse fees are exempt from the election requirement otherwise applicable to property-related fees — **confirm** this exemption is why no ratifying election was held, since it becomes central to the alternative Prop 26 "tax" theory (Section III below).
- Water Code § 71000 et seq. (Municipal Water District Law of 1911) — underlying statutory ratemaking authority and effective-date mechanics for the board resolution — **confirm** exact section(s) governing rate-resolution effective date, since that date is the trigger for every SOL discussed below.

## II. Judicial Track A — Article XIII D § 6(b) Proportionality Claim (the pled claim)

- Cal. Const. art. XIII D, § 6(b)(1)–(3) — revenue derived may not exceed funds required for the service; revenue not used for other purposes; amount imposed per parcel not to exceed proportional cost of service attributable to that parcel — the operative substantive provisions.
- Cal. Const. art. XIII D, § 6(b)(5) — burden of proof is on the **agency** to demonstrate compliance by [preponderance/clear standard — **verify exact standard of proof stated in text**].
- *Capistrano Taxpayers Assn. v. City of San Juan Capistrano* (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 1493 — governing appellate authority on tiered/conservation rate structures — **verify** the precise scope of its holding (i.e., whether it forecloses any conservation-incentive component or only requires the increment above cost differential to be independently cost-justified) before relying on it for anything beyond "cost nexus required."
- *City of Palmdale v. Palmdale Water Dist.* (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 926 — earlier tiered-rate authority — **confirm** relationship to Capistrano (whether Palmdale is subsumed/distinguished).
- Vehicle: ordinary civil action for declaratory relief (Code Civ. Proc. § 1060) and/or injunctive relief (Code Civ. Proc. § 526) — **confirm** this is the correct vehicle rather than a reverse-validation action (see Section V — this is a live, unresolved threshold question, not a formality).
- Gov. Code § 53759 (Prop 218 Omnibus Implementation Act) — **verify** whether this section (or its correct citation) imposes a 120-day statute of limitations, running from the effective date of the resolution, on judicial actions to "attack, review, set aside, void, or annul" a fee subject to art. XIII D § 6 — if it applies, **confirm the actual filing date of the ratepayer suit against the resolution's effective date.**

## III. Judicial Track B — Article XIII C / Prop 26 "Tax" Reclassification Claim (alternative theory)

- Cal. Const. art. XIII C, § 1(e) — defines "tax" broadly with seven enumerated exceptions; exception (e)(7) excludes "assessments and property-related fees imposed in accordance with the provisions of Article XIII D" — **this is the hinge**: if the surcharge fails § 6(b), it arguably falls outside exception (7) and reverts to being a "tax," making this a live alternative theory rather than a mere fallback — **confirm exact exception numbering/text in current codification.**
- Cal. Const. art. XIII C, § 1(e), final clause — **verify** whether the burden-shifting language here (agency bears burden of proving a charge is *not* a tax) is textually distinct from, or duplicative of, the § 6(b)(5) burden discussed above — both may need to be pled/argued.
- Cal. Const. art. XIII C, § 2(b) — general tax requires majority voter approval by the electorate (not the protest-hearing mechanism used here).
- Cal. Const. art. XIII C, § 2(d) — special tax (revenue dedicated to specific purposes, as here: turf rebates/debt service/education staff) requires **two-thirds** voter approval — **confirm** whether the restricted-fund earmarking described in the facts would support a "special tax" characterization if reclassification succeeds, since that changes the voting threshold that was never sought.
- Consequence if reclassified: no election of either kind occurred — **confirm** whether this is treated as a per se invalidity (as opposed to a proportionality defect subject to degree/severability arguments) — affects remedy scope, not argued here.
- SOL question: **flag** that if the surcharge is *not* "subject to Section 6 of Article XIII D" (because it's being recast as a tax outside that section), the Gov. Code § 53759 120-day period arguably does not govern this theory, and a different limitations period may apply (see catch-all SOL options in Section VI) — this bifurcated-SOL possibility is itself a major exposure/defense issue to confirm early.

## V. Validation-Action Track (Code Civ. Proc. § 860 et seq.) — threshold vehicle question, unresolved

- Code Civ. Proc. §§ 860–870 — validation procedure; if this rate resolution is a type of action a statute authorizes to be "determined" under this chapter, an agency validation action or a reverse-validation action by an interested person must be brought within **60 days** of the effective date (Code Civ. Proc. § 860; trigger and interested-person mechanics at § 863) — **confirm applicability first**, since this is not automatic for every public agency action.
- Gov. Code § 53511 — authorizes a local agency to bring a validation action regarding "bonds, warrants, contracts, obligations, or evidences of indebtedness" — **verify** whether a rate-setting resolution (as opposed to the bonds themselves) falls within this section's scope for this district.
- Water Code Division 20 (Municipal Water District Law) — **verify** whether it contains a specific clause making rate resolutions subject to CCP § 860 validation (some enabling acts do, some don't) — locate and cite the precise section if it exists.
- *Plantier v. Ramona Municipal Water Dist.* (2019) 7 Cal.5th 372 — California Supreme Court authority on whether Prop 218 rate-proportionality challenges must proceed as reverse-validation actions or may proceed as ordinary civil actions — **this is likely the controlling case on vehicle choice** and must be checked closely for its precise holding and any limiting facts before assuming either track is available/required here.
- **Consequence if validation procedure is later found to apply and the 60-day window from the resolution's effective date has passed:** potential complete bar to the § 6(b) claim regardless of merits — **confirm effective date and filing date now, not later.**
- Recycled-water-plant bond financing (30% of surcharge revenue services this debt) — **confirm** whether the bonds themselves were separately validated under CCP § 860/Gov. Code § 53511, and whether that judgment has any preclusive (res judicata) effect on challenges to the rate covenant supporting them — obtain bond validation judgment and official statement if one exists.

## X. Agencies & Forums — Jurisdiction Map

- Superior Court — sole forum with jurisdiction to adjudicate the art. XIII D § 6(b) and art. XIII C § 1(e)/§ 2 constitutional claims; no administrative exhaustion body exists for these claims themselves.
- State Water Resources Control Board — jurisdiction over water rights, curtailments, and SB X7-7/long-term-standards conservation compliance; **does not** adjudicate Prop 218 rate-proportionality disputes — confirm no parallel SWRCB proceeding is pending that could have preclusive or informative effect.
- CPUC — **confirm no jurisdiction**; municipal/public water districts are generally outside CPUC's investor-owned-utility jurisdiction.
- California Attorney General — no direct Prop 218 enforcement role (privately enforced constitutional provision); AG involvement, if any, would be collateral (e.g., UCL public-interest angle).
- LAFCO / Department of Water Resources — no adjudicatory jurisdiction over this rate dispute; DWR's role limited to conservation-plan oversight (UWMP compliance under Water Code § 10608 et seq.), not fee validity.

## Three items whose deadlines/preconditions are most easily missed

1. **Gov. Code § 53759's (or its correctly-cited equivalent) 120-day limitations period for art. XIII D § 6 challenges**, running from the resolution's effective date — easy to miss because litigants often assume constitutional claims carry no short fuse; if the ratepayer complaint was filed outside this window, the core § 6(b) claim may be time-barred even though the Prop 26 "tax" theory (arguably outside § 6's scope) may run on a different, longer clock — the mismatch between these two theories' limitations periods is itself a trap for whichever side doesn't map it early.

2. **The CCP § 860 validation-action question (Section V)** — whether this rate resolution needed to be challenged (or affirmatively validated by the district) within 60 days of its effective date. This is easy to miss because most practitioners default to treating rate-proportionality disputes as ordinary civil actions; if a reviewing court later determines validation procedure applied, an untimely reverse-validation filing can be fatal regardless of the substantive strength of the proportionality claim. *Plantier v. Ramona Municipal Water Dist.* needs to be checked closely and early, not as an afterthought.

3. **Government Claims Act presentation (Gov. Code §§ 900 et seq., 911.2, 945.6)** as a precondition to any refund/money component of relief — easy to miss because constitutional-rights litigants often assume claims presentation doesn't apply to constitutional theories; if a refund remedy is sought and no claim was presented within the 1-year window (or suit wasn't filed within 6 months of rejection), that component of relief may be barred even if the underlying constitutional theory succeeds on the merits.
