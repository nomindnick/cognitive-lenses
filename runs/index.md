## [q1 — The AI assistant as serial-meeting intermediary (Brown Act)](q1/report.html)

`Brown Act / open meetings` · **featured**

The statute was written for phone trees and go-betweens, not for software that autonomously synthesizes positions across users. Liability arguably requires deliberate use of an intermediary to develop collective concurrence — but the information flow here is exactly what the serial-meeting rule exists to prevent, and the (b)(2) staff-briefing analogy cuts both ways because staff are expressly forbidden from relaying members' views to each other. Intent, knowledge, the meaning of 'use,' and the remedy are all genuinely open.

## [q2 — Drought surcharge funding low-income rebates (Prop 218)](q2/report.html)

`Prop 218 / water rates / cost of service`

Capistrano requires tiered rates to be cost-justified, but post-Capistrano practice tolerates some incentive design; funding rebates for non-payers out of rates looks like redistribution that § 6(b)(3)–(4) forbids, yet conservation pricing has independent constitutional footing in article X, § 2, and the fee/tax/property-related characterization changes the whole analytical frame. Genuine doctrinal tension, unresolved by the California Supreme Court.

## [q3 — Councilmember, spouse's nonprofit, and the homelessness contract (§ 1090 / PRA)](q3/report.html)

`Conflicts of interest / Gov. Code § 1090 / Political Reform Act (FPPC)`

The interaction of the two regimes is treacherous: PRA recusal does not cure § 1090; a nonprofit officer's 'interest' has statutory carve-outs (§ 1091.5(a)(8)–(9), § 1091(b)(1)) whose fit here is genuinely debatable; the spouse's salary is a community-property financial interest but arguably not an interest 'in the contract' if her position is not contract-funded; and the 2019 resolution making board service an official duty supplies a colorable 'noninterest' argument. Reasonable conflicts lawyers disagree about whether this is fixable with structuring or radioactive.

## [q4 — Auto-deleting Signal chats and the franchise bid (CPRA / retention)](q4/report.html)

`California Public Records Act / records retention`

San Jose settles that public business on personal accounts can be public records but expressly left search-procedure and retention questions open; whether § 34090 reaches records the city never possessed, what 'reasonable search' means for encrypted ephemeral platforms, and what remedy exists for destroyed records are all genuinely unsettled — and the Brown Act overlay (three members in one group discussing what council will accept) is a separate live violation theory with different remedies.

## [q5 — The $1 land deal: Surplus Land Act, gift clause, and the local preference](q5/report.html)

`Surplus Land Act / gift of public funds / fair housing`

Each theory is independently debatable — the SLA exemption thresholds and covenant-length requirements are technical and HCD's interpretations are aggressive but not court-tested; gift-clause doctrine gives broad deference to recited public purposes but a $11.8M discount to a for-profit developer strains it; local preferences are common political currency yet repeatedly flagged as disparate-impact risks in demographically unrepresentative cities. The interaction (restructuring to fix one exposure can worsen another) is where the real difficulty lives.
