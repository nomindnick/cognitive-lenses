### The retrospective (assumed failure)

# Pre-Mortem: Retrospective on the "Recuse, Resign, Restructure — But It May Not Be Enough" Advice

*Reconstructed as of ~18 months post-adoption. This is a hypothetical failure narrative for angle-generation only; no finding is asserted as fact, and every citation below requires verification by counsel of record.*

The city attorney's advice was legally cautious and, on paper, defensible. It failed anyway — not primarily because the § 1090 analysis was wrong, but because a hedged, multi-condition compliance regime (recuse + resign + wall-off + restructure + maybe seek an AG/FPPC letter) is exactly the kind of advice that degrades in execution. Below are eight distinct ways it came apart.

---

**1. The "fungible funds" wall was economically illusory and got exposed**
*What went wrong:* The city required Harbor Light to certify that city/HHAP dollars would not fund the spouse's position. But Harbor Light's payroll is drawn from a single general operating account into which city contracts feed ~40%. A forensic accountant retained by a losing bidder (or the FPPC) demonstrated the certification was bookkeeping, not segregation — city money freed up other unrestricted revenue to pay the spouse regardless of the label on the check. This fungibility argument gutted the city's primary "cure" and independently supported both a § 1090 financial-interest finding and a PRA materiality finding under the *reasonably foreseeable effect on income* standard (§ 87103; cf. FPPC guidance rejecting formalistic fund-tracing where an org's budget is commingled — verify current FPPC opinion/regulation citations, likely 2 Cal. Code Regs. tit. 2, §§ 18700 et seq., post-2023 renumbering).
*Early warning sign missed:* Harbor Light's own audited financials (available at engagement) showed no restricted-fund accounting infrastructure; nobody asked whether the certification was operationally possible before relying on it.
*Likelihood:* **H** — this is a boilerplate advocacy attack on nonprofit "walls." *Severity:* **H** — it collapsed the one structural fix the advice contemplated.

**2. "Recusal" didn't survive contact with the preliminary steps**
*What went wrong:* Okafor formally abstained from the framework and award votes, but had earlier attended a budget subcommittee briefing and exchanged emails commenting on draft scoring weights before the formal recusal took effect. Under the broad "any capacity" reading of § 1090 (*Stigall v. City of Taft* (1962) 58 Cal.2d 565; *Thomson v. Call* (1985) 38 Cal.3d 633 — verify pinpoint language), participation in planning/negotiation stages taints the contract even absent a final vote. Plaintiff's counsel obtained the emails in discovery.
*Early warning sign missed:* No formal "ethical wall" memo was circulated to staff instructing them to exclude Okafor from all RFP-related communications from the moment the item was noticed, not from the moment of the vote.
*Likelihood:* **M/H** — informal contact is nearly inevitable absent an explicit, enforced wall. *Severity:* **H** — this independently voids the contract regardless of resignation or vote recusal.

**3. The board resignation came too late to matter**
*What went wrong:* Okafor resigned from the Harbor Light board, but only after the RFP framework vote (recusing from that vote rather than resigning proactively). Plaintiffs argued the § 1090/common-law conflict existed at the framework-adoption stage — arguably the more consequential stage, since scoring criteria determine who can win — and that resignation afterward doesn't retroactively cure participation-adjacent taint or the appearance-of-impropriety common-law claim.
*Early warning sign missed:* The advice recommended resignation "separately" from the vote-by-vote recusal analysis rather than sequencing it as a precondition to the framework vote; nobody set a hard deadline tied to the framework hearing date.
*Likelihood:* **M** — implementation-timing gaps are common when advice is phrased as parallel recommendations rather than an ordered checklist. *Severity:* **M/H** — creates an independent, harder-to-cure defect layered on top of #2.

**4. Council quietly overrode the "don't award absent restructuring" caution**
*What went wrong:* Facing a hard HHAP obligation deadline and political pressure not to disrupt shelter operations, the council awarded to Harbor Light on a straight recusal-only basis, treating "recuse and resign" as sufficient compliance and shelving the harder restructuring/AG-letter recommendation as impractical. The hedge in the advice ("may not be enough... absent restructuring") was heard by the client as "probably fine if Okafor recuses," because a hedged answer under time pressure gets rounded down to the action item that's easiest to execute.
*Early warning sign missed:* Counsel gave the caution in a closed-session memo but did not obtain a documented council resolution acknowledging the specific residual risk before the vote — leaving no record that the body knowingly assumed the risk (relevant later to good-faith/bad-faith characterization and to any § 1097 willfulness inquiry).
*Likelihood:* **M** — common where legal risk collides with operational urgency and the advice isn't binary. *Severity:* **H** — this is the scenario that makes every downstream remedy (voidness, disgorgement) live.

**5. A losing bidder (or taxpayer) sued after performance had begun, triggering disgorgement**
*What went wrong:* One of the other two bidders filed a reverse-validation/taxpayer action (Code Civ. Proc. § 526a — verify applicability against this contract type) roughly a year in, after Harbor Light had already drawn down a substantial share of the $2.3M and hired staff against it. The court found a § 1090 violation and treated the contract as void ab initio, ordering restitution/disgorgement of amounts paid (*Thomson v. Call*, discussing the public entity's right to recover funds paid under a void contract and the interested party's inability to recover in quantum meruit — verify holding scope). Harbor Light, having already spent the money on payroll and client services, could not repay in full, threatening its solvency and displacing active clients mid-contract.
*Early warning sign missed:* No one modeled the "we win the legal argument on remedy but the money is already spent" scenario — i.e., that timing of a challenge (post-performance, not pre-award) determines whether disgorgement is theoretical or a live operational catastrophe.
*Likelihood:* **M** — requires a motivated challenger, but a 60%-historical-incumbent losing 40% of the time gives disappointed bidders standing and motive. *Severity:* **H** — the classic § 1090 worst case: services already rendered, money already spent, no clean unwind.

**6. State HHAP funds got clawed back on top of the local mess**
*What went wrong:* Because the $2.3M was combined general-fund/HHAP pass-through money, the state's compliance review (HCFA/Cal ICH — verify current administering agency and audit authority) treated the § 1090 finding as a grant-compliance failure independent of the local litigation, and required return of the HHAP-funded portion, on top of whatever the city already owed under the disgorgement order. This effectively doubled the fiscal hit and created a second, separate headline ("state pulls homeless funding from Rio Robles").
*Early warning sign missed:* No one flagged to the state grantor, in advance, that a conflict issue was under active legal review — the state found out from the newspaper, not from the city, which read as concealment even if it wasn't.
*Likelihood:* **M** — depends on state audit timing and HHAP's own compliance posture, but combined-fund contracts routinely draw layered scrutiny once a local law-enforcement or FPPC action becomes public. *Severity:* **H** — compounds #5 and threatens future state allocations to the city generally.

**7. A whistleblower complaint produced a criminal referral, not just civil exposure**
*What went wrong:* A city staffer (or a Harbor Light employee passed over for the Director role) filed a complaint alleging Okafor's informal involvement in scoring-criteria drafting (see #2). The DA's office opened a § 1097 misdemeanor inquiry. Even though charges were ultimately declined or resulted in a plea to a lesser matter, the mere existence of a criminal investigation into a sitting councilmember over a homelessness contract became the dominant story, forced a resignation from council, and triggered a special election — a governance disruption wholly disproportionate to the underlying contract value.
*Early warning sign missed:* The advice addressed civil/void-contract risk in detail but treated § 1097 criminal exposure as a background footnote rather than a distinct, low-probability/high-visibility risk warranting its own communication plan and documentary hygiene (e.g., contemporaneous written instructions to Okafor to avoid all informal contact).
*Likelihood:* **L** — DAs rarely charge these; whistleblower-triggered referrals are the exception. *Severity:* **H** — reputational and political severity is disproportionate to legal probability, which is exactly why it's dangerous to underweight.

**8. The hedge satisfied no constituency and became its own scandal**
*What went wrong:* Homeless-services advocates framed the "don't award to Harbor Light this cycle absent restructuring" caution as the city attorney sabotaging vital shelter funding on a technicality, while good-government/transparency groups framed any continued Harbor Light involvement (recusal or not) as protected cronyism given the 60% historical win rate. The council hearing became a public spectacle with both camps attacking the same memo from opposite directions, and press coverage focused on "the city can't even competently address its own board member's conflict" rather than on homelessness policy — damaging the credibility of the RFP process generally, including for the next, unrelated procurement cycle.
*Early warning sign missed:* No public-facing explanation was prepared translating the legal hedge into plain terms before the memo (or its existence) leaked; the vacuum was filled by both narratives simultaneously.
*Likelihood:* **H** — near-certain given the profile of the facts (spouse, board seat, 60% incumbency, $2.3M homelessness money) regardless of which path the city chose. *Severity:* **M** — reputational and political, not independently existential, but it amplifies every item above.

**Read-across for counsel:** the top cluster (1–4) all point to the same structural gap — the advice specified *what* to do (recuse, resign, wall off, restructure) but not *in what sequence, with what documentary record, and with what verification that the wall was economically real* before the city relied on it. A pre-mortem-informed revision would convert the hedge into an ordered, dated checklist with an independent fungibility audit and an explicit council record of assumed residual risk — not because the § 1090 analysis was wrong, but because a conditional compliance regime this loaded with conditions fails wherever the weakest condition is, and here that was the wall.
