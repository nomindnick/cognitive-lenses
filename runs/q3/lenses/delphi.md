# Persistent Disagreement Report — Final Round

**Overall posture:** The panel converged directionally (all seven ≥ 71; median/mean 75.0; std dev 2.3). No panelist disputes the proposition's direction. The residual 8-point spread is driven not by disagreement over the conclusion but by disagreement over *where the surviving doubt lives* and *how much it is worth*. Three distinct fault lines persist.

---

## Fault Line 1: The threshold-classification question (§ 1090 "financial interest" vs. PRA-only)

**(a) Crux each side rests on**

- **Lower cluster (71, and the two 74s):** A spouse's fixed, non-contingent salary paid from a general operating budget only ~40% city-funded may fail to clear the § 1090 "financial interest … in the contract" threshold *at all*. If so, the matter is governed only by PRA §§ 87100/87103 materiality analysis — where recusal is the recognized and sufficient cure — and § 1090's no-cure-by-recusal rule never engages. The panelist at 71 explicitly notes the binary void/voidable framing does not accommodate this outcome, and one 74 states this threshold question is "where the real probability mass of 'proposition false' lives."
- **Upper cluster (77, 79):** The threshold question resolves *in favor of* finding an interest. The 77 panelist reports that spousal employment with a contracting party is treated in California municipal-conflict practice (ILG/AG guidance, Honig-adjacent reasoning) as a cognizable § 1090 interest independent of PRA analysis; the 79 panelist notes no panelist surfaced authority treating a fixed general-operating-budget salary as categorically outside § 1090.

**(b) Nature of the split:** A **genuinely unsettled legal question** — panelists on both sides characterize it as an unresolved doctrinal fork, and no one produced a verified, squarely on-point civil authority classifying this fact pattern. It is compounded by a **factual-characterization dispute over the same undisputed facts**: the 40% budget dependence / 60% win rate / director-level seniority are read by some (both 74s, 75s, 77, 79) as converting the interest into "direct and material," and by the 71 panelist as consistent with attenuation below the threshold.

**(c) What would resolve it:** A California appellate decision or AG opinion directly classifying a spouse's fixed, non-contingent salary at a nonprofit contractor as (or as not) a § 1090 interest; or new facts tying the spouse's position or compensation more directly to this specific contract (e.g., the position is contract-funded, or would be eliminated if the award were lost), which multiple panelists indicated would collapse the attenuation argument.

---

## Fault Line 2: The § 1091(b) exception-catalog question

**(a) Crux each side rests on**

- One 74 panelist keeps live the possibility that a § 1091(b) remote-interest category addressing employees of a contracting party could be **extended via imputation to spousal employment**, making the defect curable by disclosure-and-abstention without resignation.
- The 75s, 77, and 79 treat the catalog as containing no fitting category; the 79 panelist adds that under strict-construction doctrine (Stigall/Honig), unresolved ambiguity about exception fit should be resolved *toward* disqualification — a canon-allocation argument no other panelist adopted or rebutted.
- The 71 panelist accepted the "no fitting category" premise but redirected its significance: absence of an exception pushes the doubt back to Fault Line 1 rather than eliminating it.

**(b) Nature of the split:** Partly a **genuinely unsettled legal question** (scope of the enumerated categories as applied to spouses), but substantially an **evidentiary/verification gap**: multiple panelists (both 74s, 75, 77) expressly state they cannot verify the current statutory text of § 1091(b) and § 1091.5(a), or the actual holding and citation of *Fraser-Yamor Agency v. County of Del Norte*, from memory. One panelist flagged that "a meaningful part of my upward revision loses its footing" if Fraser-Yamor does not hold what the panel recalled.

**(c) What would resolve it:** Verification of the current statutory text of §§ 1091(b) and 1091.5(a); confirmation of Fraser-Yamor's actual holding; or an authority addressing whether any remote-interest category reaches spousal (as opposed to the official's own) employment with a contracting party.

---

## Fault Line 3: How to structure and price the residual uncertainty

**(a) Crux each side rests on**

- One 75 panelist *reduced* their estimate specifically because they now see two **conjunctive** uncertain steps (interest-exists AND no-exception-applies) rather than one dominant uncertainty.
- The 79 panelist held near the top of the range on the ground that this same doubt was already priced into their round-1 band and that convergence supplied corroboration, not correction.
- The 71 panelist declined to move toward the median on the stated ground that Delphi convergence "reflects shared professional priors more than a resolved authority" and that majority head-count on a contested doctrinal classification is not itself evidence.

**(b) Nature of the split:** This fault line is **not doctrinal** — it reflects **differing epistemic/risk postures**: how to aggregate stacked uncertainties, and how much evidential weight (if any) to give panel convergence itself. Panelists reached different numbers from substantially overlapping doctrinal views.

**(c) What would resolve it:** Nothing external; this is a calibration-philosophy difference. It would dissolve only if Fault Lines 1 and 2 were resolved by authority, at which point the conjunction collapses.

---

## Points of no persistent disagreement (for completeness)

- The Thomson v. Call / Stigall structural rule — recusal does not cure a sitting member's disqualifying interest — was affirmed by all seven and is treated by every panelist as the load-bearing premise.
- Community-property imputation (Fam. Code §§ 751/760) as the mechanism making the spousal salary Okafor's *own* interest was accepted panel-wide.
- The separateness of the board-seat and spousal-salary interests — and that a § 1091.5-type cure of the former would not reach the latter — drew no final-round dissent.
- One panelist noted Disagreement 4 from earlier rounds (resignation as cure) is moot under the proposition's framing, which holds board seat and employment constant; no panelist contested that.

**Bottom line:** The persistent disagreement is narrow but precise: whether an attenuated spousal-salary interest is analyzed as a *threshold* § 1090 question (potentially exiting the statute entirely into PRA territory) or as an *exception-fit* question within § 1090 — a genuinely unsettled classification that no panelist could resolve with verified authority — overlaid with an unverified-sources problem and divergent calibration philosophies. The panel's spread maps almost exactly onto how much weight each panelist assigns the threshold-classification escape route.
