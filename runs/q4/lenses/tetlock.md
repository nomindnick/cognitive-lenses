F is landscaping — helps the property value, doesn't affect the structural engineering.

## 3. Decisive Crux

**`definition-public-record` (A) is decisive for the CPRA suit as filed.**

Why it dominates the arithmetic more than its own moderate uncertainty (18 points off certainty) would suggest: it is the only node whose resolution *mechanically re-derives* two downstream nodes rather than merely nudging them. If A drops from "likely yes" to "no":
- B collapses from a live inadequate-search violation to a thin process-only objection (its own estimate's conditional split above shows B falling from ~0.80 to ~0.20 in that scenario);
- C is automatically foreclosed (it requires A as a precondition);
- D loses its main fuel — no fee-shifting-worthy merits finding, no spoliation predicate, declaratory relief shrinks to "your search methodology was generically deficient," which is a much smaller win.

No other node has that cascading, near-automatic effect on two siblings simultaneously. Even though B, C, and D each carry real independent uncertainty, their variance is *downstream of and largely explained by* A's variance — which is the textbook signature of a decisive crux in a conditional structure.

**Important qualifier — decisive for what, exactly.** A is decisive for the CPRA litigation specifically. It is *not* decisive for the city's largest dollar exposure. `brown-act-overlay` (E) sits on an independent track (E ≈ 0.78, uncorrelated with A in the strict sense) and is the one credibly connected to § 54960.1 voidability of the franchise award — a stake an order of magnitude larger than CPRA fees. If the aggregator/attorney is prioritizing where to spend investigative effort by *dollar exposure* rather than by *which single node most reorganizes the CPRA analysis*, E deserves equal or greater priority. I'm naming A as decisive per the operation's framing (the sub-question that most reorganizes the rest of this decomposition), but flagging that a second, non-subordinate decisive question exists for the larger financial question the client actually cares about.

2. **Testimonial:** Interview/depose the "uneasy" councilmember (already cooperative by virtue of having preserved screenshots) and, subject to privilege/self-incrimination considerations, the other two members and the GreenCycle consultant, on who authored substantive terms versus who merely received them.

**definition-public-record** — Do the Signal group messages fall within San Jose v. Superior Court's definition of 'public records' despite being created by a bidder's consultant, exchanged on personal devices, and largely auto-deleted? (P=82)
- Content is the dominant San Jose factor here, and it cuts strongly toward 'public record' status: franchise terms, diversion targets, RFP timing, and 'what council will realistically accept' are squarely about the conduct of public business (negotiation/award of an $180M franchise), not personal or campaign chatter — this is arguably a stronger fact pattern than San Jose itself.
- San Jose holds that writings on personal devices/accounts are 'retained by' the agency (Gov. Code former §6252(e)/current §7920.530-ish; verify current codification) when a public official prepares, sends, receives, or uses them in connection with public business — so the three councilmembers' own participation (sending, receiving, and substantively engaging) likely satisfies the test even though the channel was created and hosted by a private consultant on a third-party platform.
- A private consultant/bidder creating the Signal group and non-official participants (GreenCycle execs, advocates) being present does not, by itself, defeat classification — San Jose's inquiry focuses on the individual official's relationship to the writing (did the official prepare/receive/use it re: public business), not on who controls the platform; but messages exchanged solely among non-officials with no councilmember involvement are on weaker footing since San Jose's test applies to writings of public officials/employees.
- The one-week auto-delete setting is best treated as a retention/spoliation issue arising after the fact, not as negating public-record status at creation — 'public record' classification attaches at the moment a qualifying writing is prepared, sent, or received in connection with public business, independent of whether it later survives; this tracks the sub-question's framing and is consistent with how courts have treated destroyed/lost records in CPRA enforcement (the records existed as public records even if now irretrievable).
- Public-record status is a lower bar than disclosability: even if some messages touch on deliberative or politically sensitive strategizing ('what council will accept'), that affects exemption analysis (deliberative-process privilege, §7922.000/6255 balancing — verify current section numbers post-2021 recodification) rather than whether the writing qualifies as a 'public record' in the first instance, making the classification question easier to resolve in the affirmative than the ultimate disclosure fight.
- *Would change on:* Probability would rise toward the high 80s/90s if the surviving 40 screenshots show councilmembers actively drafting, editing, or directing franchise terms/RFP strategy (strong 'use' by officials). It would drop meaningfully (toward 50-60%) if forensic/testimonial evidence showed the three councilmembers were largely passive/silent observers who never authored or responded to substantive messages (weakening the 'prepared, owned, used, or retained by' officials element for their portion of the exchange), or if a reviewing court applied a narrower reading limiting San Jose to writings substantially authored by the official rather than merely received in a group chat controlled by a private party. Any subsequent CA appellate decision narrowing San Jose's application to third-party-hosted group chats with non-official participants would also be directly relevant and should be checked for.

**search-adequacy** — What scope of search did the CPRA require once Pacific Refuse's request expressly covered 'any platform or account,' and did the clerk's search of city systems only (without querying individual councilmembers or obtaining the 40 surviving screenshots) constitute an inadequate search as a matter of law? (P=68)
- San Jose (2017) held writings on personal devices/accounts concerning public business ARE public records, and its remand contemplated a workable protocol — e.g., the agency directing individual employees/officials to search their own accounts and submit declarations of the search performed — rather than the agency itself seizing personal devices. A search confined to city servers, when the request facially says 'any platform or account,' does not track that protocol at all, which cuts strongly toward inadequacy.
- CPRA 'reasonable search' doctrine (Gov. Code § 7922.500, formerly § 6253(c)) is generally analyzed under a 'reasonably calculated to locate responsive records' standard, often imported from federal FOIA search-adequacy cases (e.g., Weisberg v. DOJ line of authority) — courts ask whether the agency followed obvious leads, not merely whether it searched its own files. Zero outreach to the three implicated councilmembers is a strong candidate for failing that standard, especially since a search 'as a matter of law' finding is more available when the operative facts (what was and wasn't searched) are undisputed, as they appear to be here.
- Countervailing factor: whether 'as a matter of law' (vs. a triable fact question resolved after an evidentiary hearing/declarations under Gov. Code § 7923.1000, formerly § 6259) is the correct procedural framing is itself uncertain — CPRA search-adequacy disputes are frequently resolved via in camera review or sworn declarations about search methodology, which can create factual disputes about reasonableness (e.g., did the clerk have any notice of the Signal group at the time of the response?) that a court might insist on resolving before granting relief outright.
- No squarely on-point published California appellate decision has yet held, post-San Jose, that failure to contact individual officials about personal-device communications is inadequate 'as a matter of law' in every case — this remains a somewhat undeveloped doctrinal area, so characterizing the outcome as legally certain (rather than highly likely but fact-dependent) overstates current authority; the strength of this particular case (explicit 'any platform' language, existence of actual surviving screenshots proving responsiveness) makes it an unusually strong vehicle, but generalized 'as a matter of law' framing pushes past what confirmed doctrine guarantees.
- The clerk's knowledge/notice at the time of the 'no responsive records' response matters: if the city had no reason to know of the Signal group when it responded, some courts might treat the initial response as reasonable under then-known facts, with the duty to supplement arising only once Pacific Refuse's litigation or other disclosure put the city on notice — this could convert the issue into a continuing-duty-to-search question rather than a clean 'inadequate ab initio' finding.
- *Would change on:* Confirmation (or refutation) of: (1) whether the city clerk or any city official had actual or constructive notice of the Signal group's existence at the time the CPRA response was issued (e.g., from press inquiries, the uneasy councilmember's disclosure, or GreenCycle's bid materials); (2) any published post-San Jose California appellate decisions applying a concrete standard for what inquiry of individual officials CPRA requires (search terms like 'San Jose personal device search adequacy CPRA' and Government Code § 7922.500/7923.1000 case law); (3) whether the city's records-request procedures/policy already required directing employees to search personal accounts (making the clerk's failure a deviation from its own protocol, strengthening an as-a-matter-of-law finding); and (4) any trial court or writ proceeding record showing what, if anything, the clerk asked the three councilmembers before certifying 'no responsive records.'

**retention-duty-personal-devices** — Does Government Code § 34090 (or any other statutory/common-law preservation duty) extend to communications created and stored exclusively on personal devices/accounts, and did the Signal auto-delete settings — operating before any preservation duty arguably attached — violate that duty? (P=28)
- § 34090 is a *destruction-authorization* statute (requires legislative-body approval/city-attorney consent to destroy) that presupposes records are in the city's custody and control; its text and mechanics don't obviously extend to files the city never possessed on councilmembers' personal Signal accounts. San Jose v. Superior Court resolved CPRA *disclosure* scope for personal-device writings but did not hold — and left open — whether such writings become subject to a retention *schedule* adopted under §34090; no controlling CA appellate authority has closed that gap.
- Timing cuts against a clean violation: the sub-question's own premise is that most messages auto-deleted before Pacific Refuse's CPRA request or the franchise-award dispute arose. If the theory of duty is common-law spoliation, that duty typically requires litigation to be 'reasonably anticipated,' which is hard to establish for routine RFP-stage discussions months before any bid protest — though the fact one councilmember got 'uneasy' and screenshotted ~40 messages could support an argument that at least some participants had contemporaneous awareness of impropriety, arguably moving up the trigger date for those messages/that person.
- If instead the theory is that §34090's retention *period* (4 years for franchise correspondence) attaches automatically at a record's creation regardless of anticipated litigation (a purely statutory, non-spoliation theory), the timing defense is weaker — but this reading requires resolving prong one (that §34090 reaches personal-device records at all) in the affirmative first; the two prongs are not independent.
- Attribution/agency problem: §34090 and the retention schedule bind the city's records-management practices; there's no indication the city had adopted a BYOD/personal-device or messaging-app policy imposing retention duties on individual officials' personal accounts, and the disappearing-message settings were reportedly configured by a third-party consultant's Signal group, not the city — complicating any finding that the *city* (as opposed to individual members, who face separate exposure) breached a retention duty.
- This is a live, unsettled, and increasingly litigated area post-San Jose (agencies nationally are grappling with ephemeral-messaging apps and public-records/retention law); absent a squarely on-point CA case or AG opinion extending §34090 to personal-device ephemeral messaging, a court could go either way, but the structural and temporal obstacles make an affirmative, clean finding of violation less likely than not.
- *Would change on:* A California appellate decision, AG opinion, or CalOMA/Little Hoover Commission guidance squarely holding that §34090 (or a general constitutional/statutory public-records preservation duty independent of litigation anticipation) reaches personal-device communications meeting the San Jose test would raise this significantly. Conversely, authority confirming §34090 is limited to records within the city's actual custody/control, or evidence that the city had no BYOD/messaging policy and that all deletions predated any city-level knowledge of the dispute, would lower it further. Evidence pinpointing exactly when the city (not just one uneasy councilmember) had reason to anticipate a franchise-award challenge would sharpen the timing analysis for a common-law spoliation theory.

**remedies-for-destroyed-records** — Given the CPRA has no damages provision, what enforcement mechanisms are actually available for records already destroyed — declaratory/injunctive relief, CPRA fee-shifting, Penal Code § 6200 referral, or spoliation-inference/evidentiary sanctions in the parallel franchise-award challenge? (P=55)
- Declaratory/injunctive relief under Gov. Code § 7923.000 et seq. (former §§ 6258–6259) does not require that records still exist — a court can declare the search inadequate and the 'no responsive records' response unlawful, and order a renewed search (including of the 40 surviving screenshots and participants' devices) plus prospective compliance measures. This is the strongest and most likely-available mechanism, largely independent of whether the Signal texts satisfy San Jose v. Superior Court's public-records test.
- CPRA fee-shifting (Gov. Code § 7923.115, successor to § 6259(d)) is close to automatic if Pacific Refuse 'prevails,' including under a catalyst theory if the city produces the screenshots or changes its position after suit is filed. Given the clerk searched only city systems despite at least one councilmember's known participation and screenshots, an inadequate-search finding is plausible, making fee-shifting realistically available — but it is contingent on a merits finding of violation, not automatic on these facts alone.
- Penal Code § 6200 is the weak link: it targets a public officer's willful destruction of records 'required by law to be filed, deposited, or kept' with intent to defraud/injure, and case law applying it has centered on official custodians of records already in city custody, not personal-device content in a privately created, auto-deleting third-party app. Establishing that these particular texts were 'required to be kept' at the moment of deletion (before any retention duty was clearly triggered on personal accounts) and that any member acted with the requisite intent is a substantial stretch; referral is realistically available as an option but unlikely to result in a viable charge.
- Spoliation-inference/evidentiary sanctions (Evid. Code § 413; courts' inherent authority, consistent with Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. - The sub-question bundles four distinct mechanisms with materially different confidence levels; treating 'availability' as a single compound proposition (all four genuinely available and usable) yields a middling probability, even though the first two elements individually are quite likely to be found available.
- *Would change on:* Confirmation (or refutation) of: (1) whether any city official had actual knowledge of, or received/forwarded, the Signal content onto a city-controlled system (would strengthen 'use/retention by the city' and San Jose applicability, boosting declaratory/fee-shift confidence); (2) the precise timing of when litigation or a CPRA-type dispute became reasonably anticipated relative to the auto-deletions (critical to any spoliation argument); (3) any existing CA authority applying Penal Code § 6200 to personal-device or third-party-app content never transferred to public custody; and (4) whether the retention schedule adopted under § 34090 was ever construed by a court to reach personal-account communications about city business, which would materially strengthen both the retention-violation and spoliation threads.

**brown-act-overlay** — Does the three-councilmember Signal group's sustained discussion of franchise terms and 'what council will realistically accept' amount to a serial meeting or other Brown Act violation (Gov. - A real but second-order uncertainty: the facts don't specify whether the councilmembers themselves exchanged individual views/replies (strong concurrence evidence) versus mostly received talking points from GreenCycle/advocates while occasionally chiming in (weaker but likely still sufficient under the broad statutory language and intermediary theory).
- Finding an underlying violation is analytically separate from whether it can void the franchise award: § 54960.1's cure-and-correct/void remedy carries its own short limitations period and requires the challenged 'action' to be traceable to the private deliberation and later taken at an open meeting; the timing of the actual award vote relative to any cure demand is not given, so remedy/validity exposure is more uncertain than the existence of the violation itself, and § 54960 declaratory/injunctive relief may remain available even if § 54960.1 is time-barred.
- *Would change on:* The actual content of the ~40 surviving screenshots (do the three councilmembers exchange their own positions/reactions, or only read consultant/advocate messaging?); whether the thread shows real-time mutual back-and-forth among the members versus sporadic, non-responsive presence; the date of the final open-session franchise-award vote relative to Pacific Refuse's cure-and-correct demand (governs § 54960.1 timeliness and whether the vote can be characterized as the product of the private deliberation); and any evidence the consultant or advocates explicitly solicited each member's individual position and relayed it to the others (strengthening an intermediary/serial-meeting theory) versus merely broadcasting identical information to all (weakening it).
