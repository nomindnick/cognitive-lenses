### The assumed position

Counsel would likely concede that the clerk's search was legally inadequate — San Jose requires an inquiry to individual officials about potentially responsive material on personal accounts/devices, not just a search of city systems — and would recommend promptly curing this by directing councilmembers to search personal devices, producing the 40 surviving screenshots (redacted as needed for privilege/deliberative process claims that counsel would likely assert are weak but worth raising), and issuing a supplemental CPRA response rather than defending the "no responsive records" answer through litigation. On the deleted messages, counsel would resist framing auto-deletion as a knowing/willful § 34090 violation (arguing the retention schedule's applicability to individual officials' personal-device ephemeral messaging is legally unsettled and the councilmembers' conduct was not city action), while still opening a litigation hold now, quietly documenting good faith, and treating any adverse-inference or spoliation argument in the franchise litigation as a settlement-pressure factor rather than something to fight to a published ruling. Counsel would likely recommend settling/mooting the CPRA suit (supplemental production, stipulated declaratory relief, negotiated fees under Gov. ### The retrospective (assumed failure)

# PRE-MORTEM RETROSPECTIVE — Piedra Blanca Signal/CPRA Matter, +18 Months

*Hypothetical retrospective for internal red-teaming only. Written as though the failure has already occurred.*

**Bottom line, 18 months out:** The "concede the search failure, cure quietly, settle the CPRA case, quietly review Brown Act, resist the willfulness label" strategy did not contain the matter — it became the roadmap the plaintiffs and the press used to escalate it. The CPRA suit did not moot on the terms counsel expected; the franchise award itself is now under a pending order to show cause on rescission; two councilmembers have announced they will not seek reelection amid a recall filing; and a Court of Appeal opinion — the exact published ruling the settlement strategy was designed to avoid — is now citable statewide against every city that uses personal messaging apps for procurement-adjacent business.

---

## 1. Adverse-inference / spoliation ruling unwinds the franchise award

**What went wrong:** In the Pacific Refuse bid-protest and unfair-competition action, the court found the city had a duty to preserve once litigation was reasonably anticipated — arguably no later than the loss of the award and Pacific Refuse's demand letter — and that the city's litigation hold, issued only after the CPRA suit was filed, came too late to save anything. Combined with the content of the 40 surviving screenshots (RFP timing, "what council will realistically accept"), the court gave an adverse-inference instruction that the missing messages would have shown coordination on RFP timing favorable to GreenCycle. That inference, not a standalone CPRA violation, is what put the $180M award at risk of rescission/re-bid.

**Early warning sign missed:** Treating spoliation exposure as merely "a settlement-pressure factor" rather than a live evidentiary threat to the underlying procurement was itself the miss — no one asked *when* the preservation duty actually arose relative to the deletion cadence, and the fact that one councilmember spontaneously started preserving screenshots was a signal that insiders already recognized the material's evidentiary value long before the formal hold.

**Likelihood: H** **Severity: H**

---

## 2. Brown Act serial-meeting exposure surfaces from the same evidence

**What went wrong:** Three of five councilmembers discussing franchise terms over three months through a common intermediary (GreenCycle's own consultant) is a textbook fact pattern for a serial meeting under Gov. Code § 54952.2(b) (verify current subsection numbering post-recodification). The "quiet internal compliance review" did not stay quiet — it surfaced in discovery in the franchise litigation, and Pacific Refuse amended to add a Gov. Code § 54960.1 action to void the council's RFP-related actions. The instruction to avoid "volunteering a serial-meeting admission" read, once known, as consciousness of guilt rather than prudent lawyering.

**Early warning sign missed:** No one flagged that a majority-minus-one discussing substantive terms through an interested intermediary is close to the paradigm case the serial-meeting doctrine exists to catch — the review was scoped as an optics exercise, not a genuine legal-exposure assessment.

**Likelihood: H** **Severity: M–H**

---

## 3. Settlement fails to moot the CPRA case; court reaches the merits and publishes the opinion counsel tried to avoid

**What went wrong:** Counsel's strategy assumed Pacific Refuse would take a negotiated cure plus fees under Gov. Code § 7923.115 (verify current numbering) rather than press for a scope ruling. It didn't work: Pacific Refuse (backed informally by a press/CalAware amicus interest in precedent) refused to let the case moot, obtained a published Court of Appeal decision extending *San Jose*'s content/context test to hold that personal ephemeral-messaging apps used by officials for city business carry an affirmative preservation duty under Gov. Code § 34090 principles even absent city-system custody — a broader, worse-for-cities holding than the city's own concession would have required, and now binding statewide.

**Early warning sign missed:** No analysis of who besides the immediate parties had an institutional incentive to prevent mooting (transparency advocates, competing bidders in future procurements, plaintiffs' PRA bar). The strategy assumed a controllable, two-party settlement dynamic that didn't exist once the facts went public.

**Likelihood: M** **Severity: H**

---

## 4. § 1090 / Political Reform Act conflict-of-interest theory surfaces independently

**What went wrong:** Discovery revealed [hypothetically] a financial nexus between one councilmember and GreenCycle's consultant (prior consulting fees, campaign contributions, or a post-award employment discussion). That reframes the matter from a Brown Act/CPRA problem into a Gov. Code § 1090 problem — a contract in which an interested official participated in the making is void, not voidable, with no cure available (see Thomson v. Call (1985) 38 Cal.3d 633; People v. Honig (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 289 — verify pinpoints), and potentially separately implicates Gov. Code § 87100 (Political Reform Act disqualification). This theory was never on the internal review's checklist because the review was scoped around Brown Act/CPRA optics, not financial-interest due diligence.

**Early warning sign missed:** The internal "quiet Brown Act review" never asked the § 1090/§ 87100 question — whether any participating official had *any* financial relationship to GreenCycle or its consultant. Scoping the review too narrowly was the miss.

**Likelihood: M** **Severity: H**

---

## 5. "No responsive records" recharacterized as a knowing, not merely inadequate, certification

**What went wrong:** It emerged that the clerk's search was designed without first asking the individual councilmembers — and that the councilmember who had been screenshotting messages had already recognized their existence and value before the CPRA response issued. That timeline let Pacific Refuse argue the "no responsive records" answer wasn't just an inadequate-search problem under *San Jose* but a knowing false certification, eroding the city's negotiating position on fees under Gov. Code § 7923.115 and inviting a request for sanctions/referral rather than the clean supplemental-production cure counsel planned.

**Early warning sign missed:** The clerk's search methodology was never cross-checked against what individual councilmembers already knew or possessed at the time of the response — an internal timeline reconciliation that would have caught this before the answer went out.

**Likelihood: M** **Severity: H**

---

## 6. Meta-transparency failure: the containment machinery itself becomes the story

**What went wrong:** The litigation-hold memo, the "quiet" Brown Act review file, and internal settlement-strategy communications were themselves public records / discoverable material. Once litigation was filed, all of it came out — and "quiet review that avoided admitting a violation" plus "settlement to avoid a scope ruling" read in the press as a second cover-up layered on the first, independent of whether either legal position was defensible.

**Early warning sign missed:** The strategy assumed a controllable information environment (contain now, disclose later on the city's terms) that does not exist once an adverse party is actively litigating with broad discovery rights and reporters are watching a $180M procurement.

**Likelihood: H** **Severity: M**

---

## 7. Political and individual fallout: resignations, recall, referral

**What went wrong:** Once the above surfaced together, the story stopped being a CPRA technicality and became a "councilmembers took directions from a bidder's consultant on a $180M contract" narrative. Two members announced they would not seek reelection; a recall petition was filed against a third; and the screenshotting councilmember's material was referred to the DA/AG's office for evaluation under Gov. Code § 6200 (willful destruction of public records — verify current codification and elements) against the other participants, notwithstanding that no one directly ordered deletion.

**Early warning sign missed:** The plan treated this purely as a legal-risk containment exercise with no parallel political/communications risk track — no early, controlled disclosure to the full council or public despite three of five members being implicated, which is precisely the fact pattern most likely to break through to the press once any lawsuit is on file.

**Likelihood: M** **Severity: H**

---

## 8. Systemic exposure: the episode becomes a citywide "practice or policy" claim

**What went wrong:** Once the inadequate-search finding and the Signal-group facts were public, a second requester (or Pacific Refuse itself) broadened the theory to argue the city has a systemic practice of searching only city systems and never asking individual officials — seeking injunctive relief compelling a citywide search-and-certification protocol, not just a cure of this one request, under the CPRA's general enforcement provisions (verify current Gov. Code § 7923.5xx numbering, formerly § 6258–6259).

**Early warning sign missed:** No citywide audit was done of whether other departments/officials use personal messaging apps for city business before or during the response — leaving the same fragility undiscovered until tested by a follow-on requester.

**Likelihood: L–M** **Severity: M**
