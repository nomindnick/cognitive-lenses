# Structural Analysis: PB Zero Waste Future / Piedra Blanca Franchise Dispute

*Operation: structural mapping only. No conclusions offered on whether the CPRA, Brown Act, or § 34090 were actually violated — those are for the doctrinal component. This maps actors, incentives, cost allocation, and adaptive behavior.*

## 2. Who bears costs / who captures benefits, by resolution path

- **City holds the line ("no responsive records" stands, screenshots resisted):** Benefits flow to GreenCycle (keeps award, no spoliation narrative) and the two non-screenshotting members (no personal exposure). Costs fall on Pacific Refuse (litigation spend, partially offset if it prevails and fee-shifting applies — verify current codification, believed Gov. Code § 7923.115, formerly § 6259(d)) and, eventually, ratepayers (settlement/judgment costs, reputational damage to the city as an institution). The screenshotting member is put in an uncomfortable structural position: the city's litigation posture may implicitly ask them not to produce what they have, which conflicts with their personal legal exposure for withholding.

- **City discloses, admits inadequate search, reopens with custodian-by-custodian inquiry:** Reduces the city's downside on the CPRA claim itself but hands Pacific Refuse direct ammunition for the Brown Act / franchise-validity fight, which is the bigger dollar figure. Costs are political (incumbents' exposure, possible recall pressure) and fall disproportionately on the two non-screenshotting members; benefits accrue to institutional accountability and, instrumentally, to Pacific Refuse's larger case.

- **Negotiated settlement (money, re-bid concession, confidentiality) between City/GreenCycle and Pacific Refuse:** This is the modal outcome for a repeat-player defendant facing one well-resourced one-shot plaintiff — pay to make it disappear before discovery reaches personal devices generally (not just the 40 screenshots) and before an FPPC/grand jury referral becomes likely. Benefits: GreenCycle keeps the contract or a favorable substitute; Pacific Refuse gets a certain payoff instead of an uncertain declaratory judgment; city political class avoids exposure. Cost: ratepayers fund it, and the underlying practice (consultant-run ephemeral channels) is never actually disciplined — it is priced, not deterred.

- **Referral to grand jury / FPPC / DA:** Low cost to whoever refers, but weak institutional teeth (advisory reports, rare misdemeanor prosecution, nominal $500 civil penalties under the Brown Act). Its real value is reputational/narrative pressure that feeds the settlement calculus above, not a standalone remedy.

## 3. Repeat players vs. one-shotters

- **GreenCycle / its consultant** — true repeat players across many municipalities; can afford best counsel, can wait out protracted litigation, and continue to be paid under the franchise during any pendency (unless enjoined), which gives them more capacity to wait than Pacific Refuse, which loses revenue every month it isn't the franchisee.
- **Pacific Refuse** — a repeat player in the industry generally, but a one-shot, high-motivation litigant on *this* claim; likely to litigate hard and appeal because the marginal payoff ($180M-adjacent) dwarfs ordinary CPRA plaintiff economics.
- **The City** — a repeat player in the general run of low-stakes PRA requests, which is exactly why its ordinary search practices (clerk checks "city systems") were never built for an adversarial, resourced requester. Institutional muscle memory is miscalibrated for this fact pattern.
- **Individual councilmembers** — the most one-shot actors in the entire scenario: each has unique, personally consequential exposure that the city's institutional defense does not necessarily protect. Expect divergence: the screenshotting member is structurally likely to peel off toward independent counsel and cooperation, since their interests (I searched my conscience, I have a record) differ from the other two (I have nothing, and I'd like that to remain the position).

## 4. Enforcement economics — who sues, who can afford to, who never gets touched

- CPRA has no damages remedy; only declaratory/injunctive relief and one-way fee-shifting (plaintiff-favorable if they "substantially prevail" — verify current cite, believed recodified at Gov. Code § 7923.115). This means an *ordinary* requester almost never has the economic incentive to litigate a "no responsive records" answer to the point of testing *City of San Jose*'s personal-device holding — the return doesn't justify the risk/cost. Pacific Refuse is atypical precisely because it has an independent $180M commercial motive; absent that motive, this practice would in all likelihood never be tested or surfaced.
- The only reason *any* evidence survived is one individual's private unease — not an audit function, not a compliance system, not city oversight. That is a structural fact about how these violations are (or aren't) discovered: discovery here was accidental and personal, not institutional.
- **Who never gets sued and why:** GreenCycle and its consultant are not CPRA or Brown Act respondents — those statutes run against the agency/legislative body, not private lobbyists or bidders. The party that designed and profited most from the evasive channel is the party with the least direct statutory exposure under the two causes of action actually pled. Its exposure, if any, would have to come from a separate track (FPPC/Levine Act referral, or a franchise-agreement indemnification/debarment clause worth checking) — a genuinely different enforcer with different incentives and timeline, outside the plaintiff's control.
- Individual councilmembers are rarely named/personally liable in Brown Act suits (which typically target the legislative body), so their exposure is overwhelmingly political/reputational rather than legal — another mismatch between who caused the risk and who the statutory scheme is built to reach.

## 5. Institutional capacity and incentives of the enforcers/regulators themselves

- The **city clerk** has no subpoena power over a councilmember's personal phone; her "search" of city systems is the only thing she can institutionally compel. *City of San Jose* imposes an obligation to make reasonable inquiry of individual custodians, but the clerk's actual tools stop at asking — she cannot force compliance from co-equal elected officials. The gap between the doctrinal duty and the administrative capacity to discharge it is structural, not a matter of anyone's good or bad faith.
- The **city attorney** represents the entity, whose institutional interest (protect the award, minimize discovery footprint) is in tension with full, aggressive compliance — and is in outright conflict with at least one individual councilmember's personal interest. This is the kind of fact pattern where separate personal counsel for at least the screenshotting member (and arguably all three) is a live structural need, not a formality.
- **FPPC / grand jury / DA** are the only actors with independent investigatory power (subpoena, referral, potential criminal exposure under Gov. Code § 54959 for willful Brown Act violations — verify), but are chronically resource-constrained and rarely prioritize a mid-size city franchise dispute over higher-visibility matters. The formal availability of this remedy is considerably weaker in practice than it looks on paper.

## 6. What behavior each candidate rule produces once actors adapt

- **Robust remedy (spoliation inference, real fee awards, active referral):** Sophisticated repeat players don't stop the underlying coordination — they adapt the medium. Expect migration to fully non-recoverable channels (properly configured ephemeral messaging with no backup, voice-only contact, in-person meetings, consultant-as-oral-relay with no写ten record at all). A strict rule with weak-to-moderate sanctions pushes conduct further underground rather than eliminating it, because the $180M prize dwarfs the marginal legal risk.
- **Weak remedy (city's "we searched city systems" position effectively tolerated):** Formalizes a de facto safe harbor — every future bidder's consultant learns the playbook: create the group, keep it off city accounts, let disappearing messages do the retention work. Because enforcement requires a Pacific-Refuse-style plaintiff with independent motive, and even successful enforcement carries no damages and nominal Brown Act penalties, this is probably the more likely equilibrium absent this litigation.
- Either way, the deterrent asymmetry is stark: the entity that bears formal liability (the City → ratepayers) is not the entity that designed and captured the benefit of the evasive channel (GreenCycle/consultant).

## 7. Where the formal rules and the incentive structure point in different directions

*City of San Jose* holds that record status turns on content/context, not device or account. But the City's actual administrative apparatus — the clerk's search tools, the § 34090 retention schedule, city IT systems — is built entirely around *location* (city email, city servers), because that is the only thing the City can administratively control or audit. Doctrine says "look at substance"; the institution can only act on "look at custody." Genuine compliance would require building an entirely new apparatus — mandatory personal-device litigation holds, BYOD certification duties, forwarding obligations — that doesn't currently exist and that elected officials (who own their devices, aren't "employees" subject to IT policy in the ordinary sense, and value unrecorded political conversation for both legitimate and illegitimate reasons) have every incentive to resist. Any policy adopted "going forward" will only be as strong as this enforcement gap allows: a paper BYOD/retention policy without an audit or discipline mechanism changes little, and elected officials cannot be "fired" the way staff can — only recalled or criminally prosecuted, both high-friction, low-frequency events.

## 8. Practical posture implications (structural, not legal advice)

- Litigation holds should go out now to all five councilmembers, the consultant, and GreenCycle for any personal-device communications — cheap to send, high downside if omitted, and it doesn't require resolving the underlying legal question first.
- The apparent conflict between the City's institutional litigation interest and at least the screenshotting councilmember's personal interest suggests separate counsel is worth flagging early, before positions harden.
- Whether the City gets ahead of a grand jury/FPPC referral (narrative control, repeat-player reputation management) versus waits to be forced into one is itself a strategic choice with different cost profiles — worth surfacing to the client as a decision point, not a foregone conclusion.
- Any settlement calculus should account for the one-way fee-shift risk (verify current cite) and for the fact that GreenCycle, the party that designed the exposure, is not a party who can be made to bear CPRA/Brown Act costs directly absent an indemnification clause in the franchise agreement (worth pulling and checking) or an independent FPPC action.

## Non-doctrinal drivers a doctrinal analysis would miss

1. **The screenshotting councilmember's interests are not aligned with the other two, or with the City's institutional position.** Doctrinal analysis treats "the councilmembers" as a class facing a common Brown Act/CPRA question; structurally, one of them has already made a unilateral, unauthorized preservation decision that puts them at odds with colleagues and potentially with the City Attorney's official position. That fracture will shape every subsequent fact statement, declaration, and settlement posture.

2. **The real prize is almost certainly the $180M award, not the CPRA declaration.** A doctrinal reading treats the CPRA suit as the operative dispute; structurally it looks like a discovery-forcing vehicle for a Brown Act invalidation or bid-protest challenge to the franchise itself, whose limitations clock (verify Gov. Code § 54960.1 window) may already be a live strategic constraint that the parties are more focused on than the public-records question.

3. **GreenCycle and its consultant — the actors who designed and profited from the evasive channel — are structurally insulated from the two causes of action actually available (CPRA, Brown Act), which run against the agency and its members, not private bidders or their agents.** All formal cost lands on the City (ratepayers) and, reputationally, on individual councilmembers, while the architect of the arrangement faces exposure only through a separate, independently-motivated enforcer (FPPC/Levine Act, if applicable) that no party here controls.

4. **The community advocates' inclusion is a recognizable structural pattern — laundering private vendor coordination through the optics of stakeholder engagement — that is worth developing as a fact pattern regardless of what any single record turns out to be.** Doctrine asks whether messages are "public records"; it doesn't ask why two ostensibly independent advocates were sitting in a vendor's private deal-making channel in the first place.

5. **The City's institutional capacity to search personal devices is close to zero (no subpoena power over co-equal elected officials), which means the gap between what *City of San Jose* requires and what any city clerk can actually accomplish is structural, not a matter of anyone's diligence.** This has policy implications independent of who wins this case: any retention/BYOD reform adopted "going forward" will face the same enforcement-capacity ceiling unless it's paired with a real disciplinary or audit mechanism — which is politically very hard to impose on elected officials specifically.

6. **Enforcement here happened by accident (one person's private unease), not by any system designed to catch this.** That should inform how much weight the attorney gives to "this is an isolated incident" framing versus "we have no idea how often this happens because nothing here would have surfaced without a one-shot commercial plaintiff and a guilty conscience."
