### The assumed position

> Counsel would most likely treat HCD's notice of violation as the highest-probability loss — the 25-year covenant term (short of the ~45-55 year durations required under Gov. Code § 54221(f)/(g) exemption categories) and the affordability mix make the "exempt surplus land" claim weak — and would recommend curing rather than litigating: pause performance, negotiate a compliance/workout agreement with HCD (extending covenants, revisiting the mix, and/or restarting notice-of-availability procedures under §§ 54222–54223 with housing sponsors) to avoid the 30% penalty exposure. On the gift-clause claim, counsel would likely defend rather than settle, citing the public-purpose doctrine and in-kind consideration (affordable units, public plaza) as adequate under cases like *County of Alameda v. Janssen* and the deference courts give to legislative findings of public benefit, viewing this as the lowest-risk exposure. On the local-preference covenant, counsel would likely get ahead of the threatened FHA/FEHA suit proactively — narrowing the five-year preference (lower than 50%, or reframe as displacement/anti-displacement based rather than broad residency-based) before litigation is filed, since disparate-impact exposure given the city's demographics (per *Texas Dep't of Housing v. Inclusive Communities Project*) is seen as real but curable by redesign. Overall, the likely institutional posture is: cure the SLA problem administratively with HCD, defend the gift-clause claim in litigation if sued, and quietly amend the preference covenant to reduce fair-housing risk — all while advising the council that the deal is salvageable through DDA amendment rather than rescission.

### The retrospective (assumed failure)

# PRE-MORTEM: Vista Camino Disposition — Retrospective (18 Months Out)

*Invented hypothetical; no real parties. Written as a failure-already-happened retrospective for a licensed CA attorney to test and verify — not a conclusion about how the actual matter would or should go.*

**Where things stand, ex hypothesi:** The DDA was voided by court order at the request of a party the city never noticed. HCD referred the matter to the Attorney General after treating the "pause and workout" as continued noncompliance. The taxpayer suit survived summary judgment and the city took a bench-trial loss on the gift-clause claim. The fair-housing threat matured into a filed complaint alleging intentional discrimination, not just disparate impact. The council that approved the deal 4–1 no longer has a majority. Below is how the three "manageable" exposures actually interacted and metastasized.

---

## 1. A housing sponsor — not just HCD — sued to void the deal, and the HCD workout didn't stop it

**What went wrong:** The cure strategy treated HCD as the only relevant counterparty. But the Surplus Land Act's notice-of-availability requirement (Gov. Code §§ 54222–54223) exists to give *housing sponsors* a shot at the parcel, and the enforcement provisions (Gov. Code § 54230.5 — verify current section numbering post-AB 1486/SB 6) arguably give a sponsor who should have received notice an independent right to sue to void a noncompliant disposition, with fee-shifting to a prevailing plaintiff. HCD's NOV publicized the exemption defect to exactly the audience of affordable-housing developers who track HCD's compliance list. A sponsor came forward, established it would have submitted a qualifying proposal, and a court voided the DDA notwithstanding the pending HCD settlement — because HCD is not the real party in interest to that separate statutory right and can't waive it on a private party's behalf.

**Early warning sign missed:** No mapping of *which third parties* independently held enforcement rights under the Act before deciding that "cure with HCD" resolved the SLA exposure. The workout agreement's own recitals ("no NOA process was conducted") were, in effect, a roadmap for that sponsor's complaint.

**Likelihood: H — Severity: H**

---

## 2. HCD treated the "pause and negotiate" posture as continued noncompliance and referred the matter for penalty

**What went wrong:** Counsel read the NOV as a curable defect (covenant duration too short, mix wrong) rather than as a categorical rejection of the exemption claim itself. HCD's actual position was closer to "you owed a NOA process from day one" — meaning the only real cure was rescission and restart, not in-place DDA amendment. Because the city didn't rescind, didn't stop developer activity on site, and negotiated for months without a signed compliance agreement, HCD treated the interval as ongoing violation and calculated the 30% penalty against the $11.8M appraised FMV rather than the $1 price, then referred to the AG when negotiations stalled.

**Early warning sign missed:** No one tested whether HCD's NOV language was "fix the numbers" or "the entire transaction structure was unlawful ab initio." Those require different cures, and the city picked the cheaper, less disruptive reading without confirming it with HCD in writing.

**Likelihood: H — Severity: H**

---

## 3. The SLA cure record became the taxpayer plaintiffs' best evidence on the gift-clause claim

**What went wrong:** SLA cure counsel and gift-clause defense counsel were staffed as separate workstreams. The workout agreement's admissions (no NOA process, exemption improperly claimed, affordability package insufficient to qualify under § 54221) were then used by taxpayer plaintiffs to attack the *Janssen*-style public-purpose defense: if the city itself conceded the affordable-housing package didn't satisfy the statutory bar for SLA exemption purposes, that undercuts the claim that the same package was "adequate consideration" for a $11.8M-to-$1 conveyance under article XVI, § 6. Internally inconsistent positions — "the package is substantial public benefit" (gift clause) vs. "the package doesn't qualify" (SLA) — became the plaintiffs' closing argument.

**Early warning sign missed:** No single lawyer or joint-defense protocol was responsible for keeping the public record consistent across the two matters; the recitals in the HCD agreement were drafted without gift-clause counsel's review.

**Likelihood: H — Severity: M/H**

---

## 4. Pacifica could not — or would not — accept the "cure" terms, and the city had no real leverage

**What went wrong:** Extending the covenant term to the ~45–55 year range HCD implicitly required (verify exact durations under Gov. Code § 54221(f)/(g) as amended) and/or increasing the affordability mix meant altering the deal's underlying economics after Pacifica had already closed construction/LIHTC financing predicated on the original terms. Pacifica's lender and tax-credit investor covenants didn't permit unilateral term extension without new consents. Pacifica refused, then asserted anticipatory breach when the city insisted on amendment as a condition of continued performance. The city ended up choosing between (a) breaching the DDA and facing developer damages claims on a half-built site, or (b) not curing and keeping the HCD/AG exposure alive — a choice the original advice never surfaced as a real possibility.

**Early warning sign missed:** No review of Pacifica's financing documents or LIHTC allocation terms before proposing covenant extension as the fix; the "cure" was designed against the statute, not against the counterparty's actual contractual capacity to agree to it.

**Likelihood: M/H — Severity: H**

---

## 5. The "quiet" preference redesign didn't survive scrutiny — and escalated disparate impact into intentional discrimination

**What went wrong:** Narrowing the five-year preference or relabeling it "anti-displacement" without a supporting factual record (no findings tying the preference to actual displacement from *this* site — a former corporation yard, not existing housing) didn't satisfy the *Inclusive Communities*, 576 U.S. 519 (2015), burden-shifting framework's requirement of a valid interest with no less discriminatory alternative. The fair-housing organization sued anyway, and discovery into the redesign process — done for litigation-avoidance rather than grounded in AFFH-consistent findings (Gov. Code § 8899.50 — verify application here) — surfaced internal communications treated by plaintiffs' counsel as evidence of intent, converting the claim under *Village of Arlington Heights v. Metro. Housing Dev. Corp.*, 429 U.S. 252 (1977), into intentional discrimination, exposing the city to compensatory/punitive damages and fee-shifting under 42 U.S.C. § 3613(c)(2) that a pure disparate-impact theory would not have carried.

**Early warning sign missed:** The fair-housing org's demand letter almost certainly previewed exactly what evidentiary showing (displacement data, need findings) it would require to accept a redesign; the redesign didn't engage with that showing because it was built to reduce optics/percentage exposure, not to meet the legal standard.

**Likelihood: M — Severity: H**

---

## 6. The "quiet" DDA amendment was itself a Brown Act violation, converting a legal problem into a governance scandal

**What went wrong:** Amending a publicly approved 4–1 disposition and development agreement via staff-level side letter or closed session (rather than a noticed open-session council action) likely violated the Brown Act (Gov. Code § 54950 et seq.), giving rise to a cure-and-correct demand or validity challenge under § 54960.1. Local press framed it as "city tried to quietly fix a housing discrimination problem behind closed doors while also facing a $3.5M state penalty" — a story that outlasted and outweighed the underlying legal merits, poisoned the possibility of a negotiated resolution with the fair-housing org (who now assumed bad faith), and fed the recall effort described below.

**Early warning sign missed:** No Brown Act sign-off was attached to the "narrow the preference quietly" recommendation before it went to staff for implementation.

**Likelihood: M — Severity: M/H**

---

## 7. Political collapse: the council majority didn't survive the 18 months, and the cure was repudiated mid-course

**What went wrong:** The original approval was 4–1 — a contested vote from the start, on a deal headlined as "$11.8M property sold for $1 in a city that's 78% white against a 52% regional baseline." Once the HCD penalty, taxpayer suit, and fair-housing complaint were all simultaneously public, a recall qualified against two councilmembers and a third lost re-election. The new majority repudiated the workout agreement negotiated by the prior council, leaving the city in breach of both the HCD settlement and (per #4) potentially the DDA — worse off than either fighting or fully unwinding at month one would have left it.

**Early warning sign missed:** The strategy was risk-mapped legally but not politically; no plan existed for maintaining council support through an 18-month, multi-track cure process, and the single dissenting vote was never treated as a leading indicator of fragility.

**Likelihood: M/L — Severity: H**

---

## 8. Collateral consequence: the open HCD violation made the city ineligible for unrelated state funding

**What went wrong:** Several state housing/infrastructure programs (e.g., HCD's Prohousing Designation Program, and AHSC/infill infrastructure grant cycles — verify current program rules) condition eligibility on SLA compliance certification. With an open, unresolved NOV, the city's pending applications for unrelated infrastructure and housing funds were deemed ineligible, a consequence nobody modeled when scoping the "cure" as a bilateral HCD problem.

**Early warning sign missed:** No cross-check of the city's other pending state grant applications against SLA-compliance certification requirements before deciding a slow-walked workout was low-cost.

**Likelihood: M — Severity: M**

**Cross-cutting takeaway for verification:** The three tracks (SLA, gift clause, fair housing) were advised as if severable — cure one administratively, defend one in court, quietly fix one by redesign. The dominant failure pattern above is that these are *evidentiarily and politically coupled*: action taken in one track generated the record, the leverage failure, or the public narrative that broke another. Any restructuring plan should be pressure-tested against (a) third-party enforcement standing beyond HCD, (b) the counterparty's actual contractual/financing capacity to accept the proposed cure, (c) a single controlled factual record across all three matters, and (d) open-session/Brown Act compliance for any amendment, before it is presented as "salvageable through DDA amendment."
