## The question

The City of Vista Camino (invented; demographics roughly 78% non-Hispanic white against a metro region at 52%) owns a 4.2-acre former corporation yard appraised at $11.8M. The council approved, 4–1, an exclusive negotiating agreement and then a disposition and development agreement conveying the parcel to Pacifica Communities LLC, a private developer, for $1, in exchange for: 120 housing units of which 30% (36 units) are deed-restricted affordable — 18 at low-income and 18 at moderate-income levels — with 25-year covenants; a half-acre publicly accessible plaza built and maintained by the developer; and a "community preference" covenant giving 50% of the affordable units priority to applicants who have lived or worked in Vista Camino for at least five years.

The city declared the parcel "exempt surplus land" and did not send notices of availability to housing sponsors before entering exclusive negotiations. The Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD) has issued a notice of violation asserting the disposition does not qualify for the claimed exemption (citing the affordability mix and the 25-year covenant term) and warning of penalties of 30% of the disposition price or fair market value. A taxpayer association has sued alleging an unconstitutional gift of public funds (Cal. Const. art. XVI, § 6) given the $11.8M-to-$1 spread, and a regional fair-housing organization has threatened suit alleging the five-year local preference has an unlawful disparate impact given the city's demographics.

**Asked:** Does the disposition violate the Surplus Land Act (Gov. Code §§ 54220–54234) — was the parcel eligible for any 'exempt surplus land' category given the 30% low/moderate mix and 25-year covenants, what process was owed, and what do HCD's enforcement position and the 30% penalty mean for the deal? Is $1 plus in-kind public benefits adequate consideration under the gift clause, or is the public-purpose doctrine enough? Does the local-preference covenant violate the FHA/FEHA under a disparate-impact theory or AFFH obligations, and can it be saved by redesign? How do the three exposures interact, and how should the deal be restructured?

**Delphi proposition:** "If challenged as structured, the disposition would be held unlawful under the Surplus Land Act (i.e., HCD's position that the parcel did not qualify as exempt surplus land and that the required process was violated would substantially prevail)."

**Why it's contested:** Each theory is independently debatable — the SLA exemption thresholds and covenant-length requirements are technical and HCD's interpretations are aggressive but not court-tested; gift-clause doctrine gives broad deference to recited public purposes but a $11.8M discount to a for-profit developer strains it; local preferences are common political currency yet repeatedly flagged as disparate-impact risks in demographically unrepresentative cities. The interaction (restructuring to fix one exposure can worsen another) is where the real difficulty lives.

## 1. Argument map

### Convergent (multiple independent lenses agree)

- **The affordability mix fails the claimed exemption under every version of the threshold anyone recalled**: 30% of units restricted overall, but only 18/120 (15%) at the strictly "lower income" tier; moderate-income units (up to ~120% AMI per Health & Saf. Code § 50093, unverified) likely do not count toward the exemption's numerator. [delphi, tetlock, dialectic, steelman]
- **The 25-year covenant term is an independent defect**, well short of the ~45/55-year durational floors the lenses recall from § 54221(f) and analogous regimes (density-bonus law, LIHTC) — and the covenant's year-26 reversion of value to the developer is separately flagged as the economic core of HCD's objection. [delphi, tetlock, dialectic, steelman, structural]
- **The skipped § 54222 Notice of Availability before the ENA is, on the stated facts, a violation** — total omission, not defective notice. [delphi, tetlock, dialectic, steelman, premortem]
- **No alternative § 54221(f) exemption category plausibly covers a $1 conveyance of a 4.2-acre corporation yard to a for-profit LLC for a majority-market-rate project.** [delphi, tetlock, dialectic]
- **HCD's core characterization (not exempt; process owed and skipped) very likely substantially prevails**: delphi panel median 82 (range 80–84); tetlock estimated only 8% that any exemption applies; dialectic's synthesis lands the same way; premortem's assumed baseline treated the SLA claim as the highest-probability loss; steelman constructs the full-strength version of the same conclusion. [delphi, tetlock, dialectic, premortem, steelman]
- **Liability and remedy are different questions**: a violation finding is far more certain than rescission/unwind of the DDA; the Act may supply penalty-plus-compliance relief rather than an express rescission remedy. [delphi, tetlock, dialectic]
- **The 30% penalty base is likely the greater of price or FMV → roughly $3.54M**, not 30% of $1 — which is what gives the NOV teeth. [delphi, tetlock, dialectic, premortem, steelman]
- **Every statutory threshold in the analysis is recalled from memory, unverified across the AB 1486 / AB 1255 / SB 6 / AB 2135 / AB 480 amendment cycles** — self-flagged by every delphi panelist and by both dialectic components' verification checklists. [delphi, tetlock, dialectic]
- **The SLA violation feeds the gift-clause claim evidentially**: HCD's finding (or the city's own cure-record admissions) is the taxpayer plaintiffs' best pretext/inconsistency evidence against Janssen-style public-purpose deference, even though the doctrines are formally separate. [tetlock, dialectic, premortem, steelman]
- **Janssen-line deference to legislative public-purpose findings — not dollar-for-dollar proportionality — is the governing gift-clause frame and the city's principal defense.** [tetlock, dialectic, premortem]
- **The disparate-impact exposure on the five-year preference is real, governed by the *Inclusive Communities* burden-shifting framework, with AFFH/AB 686 (Gov. Code § 8899.50, unverified) operating as an independent source of pressure regardless of litigation outcome.** [tetlock, steelman, premortem]
- **The absence of a displacement nexus (a corporation yard displaced no residents) weakens the anti-displacement justification; a displacement/right-to-return-based preference is the safer design.** [tetlock, premortem, steelman]
- **The three exposures' fixes do not map 1:1**: deepening affordability/extending covenants helps SLA and gift-clause simultaneously (synergistic); the preference fix is orthogonal; and re-running the NOA process collides with deal finality/Pacifica's reliance — the one potentially irreducible tension. [tetlock, structural, premortem, steelman]
- **The decisive uncertainties are documents, not doctrine**: verified current § 54221(f) text, the city's actual resolution/exemption finding, and ENA/DDA dates would collapse most residual uncertainty. [delphi, tetlock, dialectic]

### Contested (lenses genuinely conflict)

- **NOA violation: freestanding second ground vs. derivative consequence.** Majority of the delphi panel (≈5 of 7) and the dialectic thesis treat the skipped notice as an independently sufficient violation; a delphi minority and the dialectic antithesis treat it as parasitic on the exemption determination (one legal question, two labeled consequences); the dialectic synthesis relabels it "one liability determination plus aggravating sequencing evidence." [delphi, dialectic] — *Nature: legal question about statutory architecture; no lens disputes the outcome direction.*
- **What "substantially prevail" encompasses.** Delphi's lower cluster and the dialectic antithesis read it as importing remedy-stage outcomes (cure, substantial compliance, penalty-only resolution); delphi's upper cluster and the dialectic synthesis read the proposition's parenthetical as confining it to the merits characterization. [delphi, dialectic] — *Nature: proposition-interpretation, layered over a genuinely unsettled remedies question under the SLA.*
- **Gift clause outcome.** Tetlock estimates the taxpayer suit fails more often than not (~27% standalone, ~30–40% adjusted for SLA coupling) given deference; the steelman constructs a full case that the $1 conveyance is an actual unconstitutional gift (no reversion, no ground lease, no appraisal-documented offset; distinguishing *Dockweiler* and *Elliott*, all unverified); premortem depicts the city losing at bench trial after its own SLA-cure record contaminates the defense; structural adds that the appraisal spread itself may be overstated. [tetlock, steelman, premortem, structural] — *Nature: partly legal question, partly by-design role assignment (steelman deliberately builds the disfavored pole); premortem's version turns on a controllable factual predicate (record consistency).*
- **Can the preference be saved by redesign?** Tetlock, structural, and premortem's assumed baseline treat redesign (narrower share, displacement-based, sunset) as the likely fix; the steelman argues dilution is the same unlawful mechanism at smaller scale and only elimination or de-linking from local tenure works; premortem adds that redesign done for optics without a factual record risks escalating disparate impact into an intentional-discrimination claim in discovery. [tetlock, structural, premortem, steelman] — *Nature: legal question plus a missing factual predicate (applicant-pool demographics, displacement history).*
- **Likely real-world trajectory: negotiated cure vs. cure-path failure.** Dialectic's antithesis and structural argue every repeat player's incentives point to a negotiated, never-adjudicated compliance resolution; premortem stress-tests exactly that path and has it fail (HCD treats "pause and negotiate" as continued noncompliance; a sponsor sues to void; the developer cannot accept the cure terms); the steelman argues the penalty is mandatory and only a full restart complies. [dialectic, structural, premortem, steelman] — *Nature: risk-tolerance/behavioral prediction, hinging on an ascertainable fact — whether the NOV demands "fix the numbers" or treats the structure as unlawful ab initio.*
- **Significance of no competing sponsor appearing.** Dialectic's antithesis argues the absence of any sponsor claiming it would have bid weakens the case for strong relief (prejudice analogy); premortem's top-ranked failure mode is a sponsor emerging *because* the NOV publicized the defect. [dialectic, premortem] — *Nature: factual-assumption conflict about third-party behavior.*
- **Epistemic weight of convergence-despite-divergent-recollections.** Within delphi, the upper cluster treated seven incompatible threshold reconstructions all yielding "fails" as cross-validating robustness; the low anchor discounted it because convergence among people sharing the same unverified memory is weaker than verification. [delphi] — *Nature: pure epistemic risk-tolerance; the delphi report itself flags this as unresolved.*

### Unique (surfaced by a single lens)

- ***Yamaha* deference may cut against HCD**: an NOV is a litigating position, not longstanding guidance; APA-exempt guidelines lack notice-and-comment vetting; and deference is irrelevant to bare numeric comparisons anyway. [dialectic]
- **Timing/retroactivity gap**: nothing in the facts fixes ENA/DDA dates against AB 1486 / SB 6 operative dates or any savings/transition clauses; a pre-amendment standard could change the applicable thresholds (*Evangelatos* presumption, unverified). [dialectic]
- **Validation-action limitations bar possibility** (CCP §§ 860–870, unverified, flagged as a reach). [dialectic]
- **"Sole purpose" wrinkle**: the plaza as bargained-for consideration may undermine the exemption's sole-purpose element — and its analysis turns on the DDA's actual title/easement treatment of the plaza. [dialectic]
- **Penalty-canon tension**: remedial-statute broad construction vs. narrow construction of penalty-triggering provisions (*Kizer* analogy, unverified). [dialectic]
- **Composite exposure arithmetic**: ~93–94% chance at least one of the three tracks produces real exposure; penalty exposure is bimodal (near-nominal vs. ~$3.5M-scale), and the lens itself warns its $1.8M "expected exposure" figure must not be quoted as a damages estimate. [tetlock]
- **Counterparty capacity as the hidden constraint on cure**: Pacifica's lender/LIHTC consents may bar covenant extension, converting a "cure" into anticipatory-breach exposure. [premortem]
- **Brown Act exposure** if the preference/DDA amendment is done "quietly" rather than in noticed open session (§ 54960.1 cure-and-correct risk). [premortem]
- **Collateral state-funding ineligibility**: an open NOV may disqualify unrelated Prohousing/AHSC-type applications (program rules unverified). [premortem]
- **Intentional-discrimination escalation**: discovery into an optics-driven redesign can convert disparate impact into an *Arlington Heights* intent claim with damages and fee-shifting exposure (42 U.S.C. § 3613(c)(2), unverified). [premortem]
- **HCD internal silos**: the SLA enforcement function and the housing-element/RHNA function may be uncoordinated — the city may be claiming RHNA credit for the same project from one arm of the agency penalizing it through another. [structural]
- **Funding-source opacity of both plaintiff organizations** as a discovery/strategy priority (taxpayer suits recover no damages; someone is paying). [structural]
- **The appraisal is a contested input, not a given**: former-corporation-yard contamination/remediation liability and the uncosted plaza obligation may substantially shrink the real $11.8M-to-$1 spread. [structural]
- **The statute's intended beneficiaries are structurally voiceless**: excluded sponsors don't know they were harmed; the 36 future tenant households bear the cost of every resolution path via delay, and no party is optimizing against that. [structural]
- **Behavioral adaptation risks**: aggressive SLA enforcement may push cities toward market-rate sales or leases (less affordable housing, not more); a gift-clause win on a bare price-spread theory would chill the standard nominal-price-plus-covenant subsidy mechanism statewide. [structural]
- **Personal-exposure asymmetry**: council members face no individual financial consequence (legislative immunity/indemnification, unverified), which structurally encourages legally aggressive dispositions. [structural]
- **Reverse-engineered exemption hypothesis**: if the ENA predates any documented exemption analysis, the finding was fit to a pre-selected partner — discoverable through a timeline/document review. [structural] *(delphi and dialectic separately flag "whether the city documented an exemption finding before the ENA" as a resolving fact, but only structural frames it as the intent/process-integrity theory.)*
- **Affirmative restructuring toolkit for the gift-clause problem**: ground lease preserving fee, 55/45-year covenants, shared-appreciation/recapture, appraisal-documented offset pricing — plus *Parma*-style precedent (unverified) against homogeneous-suburb residency preferences. [steelman]
- **Penalty as mandatory rather than negotiable** — treating the 30% as a bargaining chip reintroduces the pre-2019 softness the reform eliminated. [steelman]

## 4. Noise audit

The genuine payload is compact: the convergence-despite-incompatible-recollections pattern, the persistent-disagreement report (especially the "substantially prevail" and freestanding-vs-derivative splits), and the shared self-flag that every threshold is unverified memory. Roughly 80% of the lens's word count could be cut without losing signal.

The crux decomposition and fan-out table are high-signal.
- **[dialectic]: substantive but long, with internal duplication.** The thesis and antithesis verification checklists substantially overlap each other and delphi's authority list; the synthesis is the highest-value component and does real work resolving the remedy/merits confusion.
- **[steelman]: not noise, but read with its assignment in mind.** It is by design one-directional advocacy for the disfavored pole; its rhetorical framing ("insider deal," "penalty is owed, not a bargaining chip") exceeds its evidentiary support, and its distinguishing of *Dockweiler*/*Elliott* and reliance on *Parma* are unverified.
- **[premortem] and [structural]: high signal, minimal duplication** — these two lenses contributed most of the unique, non-overlapping content in the pipeline.
- **Cross-cutting caution:** the delphi and tetlock probability figures are independent subjective estimates converging on the same qualitative conclusion; that convergence is meaningful, but the numbers themselves should not be quoted downstream as calibrated forecasts. [delphi, tetlock — per their own caveats]

## 1. Surplus Land Act (Gov. Code §§ 54220–54234)

### 1.1 What process was owed

Before entering into an ENA or any disposition negotiation, a local agency must, at an open public meeting, make a written finding classifying the property as either:

- **"Exempt surplus land"** (one of the enumerated categories in § 54221(f)(1)) — if so classified, the notice/priority-negotiation machinery below doesn't apply, but the agency must still describe and document the exemption basis, and HCD can (and here did) dispute it; or
- **"Surplus land"** (non-exempt) — which triggers:
  1. A **Notice of Availability (NOA)** to every housing sponsor on HCD's registry and to specified local/park/school entities *before* the agency may negotiate a disposition;
  2. A **60-day priority negotiating window** during which any qualifying entity that responds — affordable-housing developers first, then park/open-space and school entities — gets exclusive good-faith negotiation rights ahead of a private market-rate buyer;
  3. If the land is ultimately sold for housing to a non-priority buyer, a **statutory floor** requiring not less than roughly 15% of units be affordable to *lower-income* households under an enforceable, recorded restriction;
  4. Reporting of the disposition terms to HCD, which has review and objection authority.

The city did none of the notice/priority-negotiation steps. It went straight to an ENA with Pacifica on the strength of a self-declared exemption. That means the procedural defect exists **independent of** whatever the substantive exemption analysis shows: even a perfectly-designed affordable housing deal entered without an NOA and 60-day window is a violation, because other priority entities (housing sponsors, the school district, a parks agency interested in that half-acre plaza site) never got the statutory chance to compete for the parcel.

### 1.2 Was any exemption actually available?

No — on the facts as given, for two independent reasons, and HCD's notice of violation is targeting exactly these two:

**(a) Income mix.** The affordable-housing exemption categories in the Act are keyed to units affordable to *lower-income* households (Health & Safety Code §§ 50079.5/50105 definitions), not "low or moderate" as a blended category. Moderate-income (up to ~120% AMI) units **do not count** toward the exemption threshold. Here, only 18 of 120 units — **15%**, not the advertised 30% — are true lower-income units. That falls short of whatever the applicable mixed-income exemption threshold is (the statute, post-AB 1486/AB 480, requires a meaningfully higher lower-income percentage, commonly cited around 25%, for that pathway to apply — verify the current figure against the codified text, as this provision has been amended repeatedly). Nor does the project qualify for the more generous **100%-affordable exemption**, since only 15% of units are lower-income restricted. The city's "30% affordable" framing is doing real rhetorical work to disguise a 15% lower-income project — precisely the kind of characterization HCD's enforcement program was built to catch.

**(b) Covenant term.** Every affordability-based exemption category requires a recorded deed restriction of **not less than 55 years for rental housing** (45 years for ownership). A 25-year covenant is independently, and unambiguously, fatal — it isn't a close call. Even if the income mix were fixed, a 25-year term alone forecloses exemption eligibility.

**Conclusion:** the parcel was not eligible for exempt surplus land status under any affordability-based category. HCD's position is legally sound on these facts.

### 1.3 Consequences

- **Penalty exposure.** Civil penalties under the Act run 30% of the greater of the disposition price or fair market value for a first violation, 50% for subsequent/continuing violations. Sale price here is $1, so the base is FMV: **30% × $11.8M ≈ $3.54M**. If the city proceeds without curing after HCD's notice, the 50% tier becomes a live risk (~$5.9M).
- **Voidability / injunctive exposure.** The Act creates a private right of action allowing an interested person (and separately, HCD via referral to the Attorney General) to seek to enjoin, or void, a disposition made in violation of the Act, in addition to compelling compliance. Combined with the taxpayer suit already filed, this is a real risk to the transaction's finality, not just a fine.
- **Practical posture with HCD.** HCD notice-of-violation letters typically open a negotiation for a corrective action plan before AG referral. That's the leverage point — and the reason curing quickly matters more than litigating the exemption question.

*(Caveat: subsection lettering and exact percentage/threshold figures in § 54221(f) have been amended several times — AB 1486 (2019), AB 1255/SB 6 (2019), AB 2135 (2022), AB 480 (2023) — confirm against current codified text before finalizing any filing. The substantive conclusions above — lower-income-only counting, 55/45-year minimums, the ~15% floor, and the 30%/50% penalty structure — are well-settled features of the Act and are not in serious doubt.)*

## 2. Gift of public funds (Cal. Const. art. XVI, § 6)

### 2.1 The doctrine, correctly framed

Article XVI, § 6's prohibition on gifts of public funds is analyzed under a **highly deferential** two-part inquiry, not ordinary contract-law "adequacy of consideration":

1. **Public purpose test** (*County of Alameda v. Carleson* (1971) 5 Cal.3d 730): if any legitimate public purpose can fairly be conceived for the expenditure, courts will not second-guess the legislative body's judgment about value — "the courts have no power to interfere" absent a showing the purpose is a sham.
2. **Consideration** matters mainly as *evidence*: gross disproportion between value given and value received can suggest the stated public purpose is pretextual, but courts do not require dollar-for-dollar equivalence. Non-cash, legally enforceable consideration — recorded covenants running with the land, construction and maintenance obligations — counts as real consideration (compare the pension-as-consideration reasoning in *County of Alameda v. Janssen* (1940) 16 Cal.2d 276; subsidized-housing-as-public-purpose reasoning in *California Housing Finance Agency v. Patitucci* (1978) 22 Cal.3d 171).

**Direct answer to the question posed:** you don't need both prongs independently satisfied — the public-purpose doctrine, standing alone, is normally sufficient to defeat a gift-clause claim, *provided* the disproportion isn't so extreme it reveals the "public purpose" as pretext. $1 plus enforceable in-kind obligations (affordable units, plaza construction and maintenance) is very likely to be treated as adequate consideration under this framework — this is the same structural logic that has long sustained redevelopment-era land write-downs to private developers in exchange for public benefits.

### 2.2 Why this deal is more exposed than the doctrine's usual comfort level

The vulnerability here isn't the doctrine — it's the facts feeding into it. HCD has effectively found, as a matter of state housing law, that the "affordable housing" benefit the city is citing as its public purpose is smaller and shorter-lived than advertised (15% true lower-income, 25-year term, not 30%/permanent-equivalent). A taxpayer plaintiff will argue: *the very benefit the city claims justifies an $11.8M-to-$1 spread doesn't even satisfy the state's own statutory floor for what counts as adequate affordable-housing consideration for surplus public land* — which is a much more concrete, judicially cognizable argument than a generic "$11.8M for $1 smells wrong" claim. The SLA finding doesn't automatically become a gift-clause violation, but it substantially strengthens the plaintiff's "sham public purpose" narrative and weakens the city's evidentiary record.

Conversely, curing the SLA problems (raising true lower-income percentage, extending the covenant to the 55-year minimum) directly strengthens the gift-clause defense — it makes the in-kind consideration larger, more durable, and independently validated by the state housing regulator.

### 2.3 What actually protects the city here

- **Contemporaneous, specific findings**: a council resolution making explicit findings on (a) the public purpose served (remediation of an underused corp yard, RHNA/housing-element production, publicly accessible open space), and (b) why the exchange is reasonably related to value given — ideally supported by an appraiser's **gap/residual-land-value analysis** quantifying the developer's forgone market rent over the covenant term plus plaza construction/maintenance cost. This doesn't need to equal $11.8M; it needs to show the city reasoned it through rather than waved it through.
- **Enforceable recourse**: recorded covenants pre-closing, completion guaranties/bonds for the plaza, and reversion rights if affordability or maintenance obligations lapse. Courts treat retained enforceable recourse as evidence the "consideration" is real, not illusory.
- **Procedural regularity**: SLA compliance (or a documented cure) is not a legal safe harbor for gift-clause purposes, but it is powerful evidentiary support for "this was a regular, deliberated public-benefit exchange," which is exactly what defeats a "sham" argument.

**Bottom line:** the gift-clause claim, litigated today on these facts, is more likely than not to survive a motion but is not a slam-dunk win for the city given the SLA overlay; after the SLA/preference cures recommended below, it becomes a comfortable win.

## 3. The five-year local-preference covenant — FHA/FEHA disparate impact and AFFH

### 3.1 Framework

- **FHA**: disparate-impact liability for facially neutral housing policies is settled federal law (*Texas Dept. of Housing & Community Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project* (2015) 576 U.S. 519), using a burden-shifting test: (1) plaintiff shows a robust statistical/causal disparity from the challenged policy; (2) defendant shows the policy is necessary to a substantial, legitimate, nondiscriminatory interest; (3) plaintiff may still win by showing an equally effective, less discriminatory alternative exists.
- **FEHA**: Gov. Code § 12955.8 codifies the same disparate-impact framework for California housing discrimination claims.
- **AB 686 / AFFH duty**: Gov. Code § 8899.50 imposes an *affirmative* duty on every public agency to administer housing-related activities — expressly including disposition of publicly owned land for housing — in a manner that affirmatively furthers fair housing. This is broader and more proactive than disparate-impact avoidance; it's a design obligation, not just a litigation defense, and it gives HCD an independent hook (on top of the SLA violation) to scrutinize this disposition.

### 3.2 Application

The 78%-white-city/52%-metro-region gap is a real signal, but plaintiffs will need **applicant-pool-level data** (not just general population figures) to satisfy *Inclusive Communities*' "robust causality" requirement — general demographics alone are suggestive, not sufficient, though they're enough to make this a credible threatened suit rather than a nuisance claim. The design features that make this preference especially exposed:

- It reaches **50% of the affordable units** — a large carve-out, not a modest tie-breaker;
- The qualifying criterion is a **generic durational residency-or-employment test**, unconnected to any actual displacement caused by this project or the corp-yard's prior use;
- It operates in a jurisdiction whose demographics already diverge sharply from the region it draws applicants from.

HUD and fair-housing litigation practice have long treated **broad, undifferentiated local/neighborhood preferences** in racially disparate jurisdictions as high-risk (this is the same fact pattern that has generated real-world HUD scrutiny of neighborhood-preference lotteries in segregated markets). The city's likely defenses — jobs-housing balance, community stability, political support for the project — are legitimate interests in the abstract, but a **generic 5-year test with no displacement nexus** is a weak fit for the "necessary to achieve" step, and is vulnerable at the "less discriminatory alternative" step because far more narrowly tailored designs exist (below).

### 3.3 Can it be saved by redesign? Yes — but not left as-is

Redesign options, roughly in order of legal defensibility:

1. **Convert to a true anti-displacement / right-to-return preference** — limited to households or individuals actually displaced by this project or the corp yard's operations. This is the best-established, most defensible model.
2. **Reduce the share and convert from an absolute set-aside to a tie-breaker** applied only among otherwise equally qualified applicants, rather than a screen that excludes non-local applicants outright.
3. **Reconsider the geographic/eligibility definition** (e.g., tie to specific local employers/essential-worker categories rather than generic residency) — but test any redesign against real applicant-pool data before adopting it; don't assume it fixes the problem.
4. **Add affirmative, region-wide marketing obligations** targeted at protected-class populations to counteract the pool-narrowing effect of any preference retained.
5. **Commission a written AFFH/disparate-impact assessment before re-adoption** — this both satisfies the AB 686 obligation directly and builds the administrative record that will matter if litigation follows.
6. **Check program-specific caps.** If the project uses 4%/9% LIHTC (likely, given the affordability structure), CTCAC regulations independently constrain local preferences — typically capping them well below 50% and requiring a displacement or comparable nexus. That cap applies regardless of how the FHA/FEHA analysis comes out, and the current 50% figure is likely non-compliant with those program rules on its own terms if tax credits are in the capital stack.

**Bottom line:** the preference as drafted is a genuine, well-founded exposure — not a nuisance claim — but it is readily curable through narrowing and an AFFH-grounded redesign, at comparatively low cost, before litigation is filed.

## 4. How the three exposures interact

These are not three independent risk silos — they reinforce each other in both directions:

- **SLA → Gift Clause:** HCD's finding that the "affordable housing" benefit doesn't meet the state's own floor (income mix, covenant term) directly undercuts the city's public-purpose/consideration narrative for the taxpayer suit. Curing the SLA defect enlarges and validates the in-kind consideration, which is the single most useful thing the city can do for its gift-clause defense.
- **Preference → SLA and Gift Clause:** A discriminatory design feature in the disposition is an aggravating fact HCD can cite (AFFH duties attach to land-disposition decisions specifically), and it undermines the "legitimate, unqualified public purpose" characterization a court needs to reject the gift-clause claim. It's hard to argue the deal serves an unimpeachable public purpose while defending a feature that predictably produces a whiter beneficiary pool than a regionally open process would.
- **Practical sequencing risk:** if the SLA violation leads to voiding or unwinding the disposition, the gift-clause and fair-housing claims may become moot for *this* transaction — but the city then faces developer breach exposure under the DDA (if closing conditions didn't anticipate this), the 30%/50% penalty, and the reputational/political cost of restarting. The far better path is curing in place rather than litigating the exemption question or waiting for a court to unwind the deal.

## 5. Recommended restructuring

1. **Do not close (or, if already recorded, pause performance and open a cooperative dialogue with HCD)** — a corrective-action posture, not a defend-the-exemption posture, is the right frame given the strength of HCD's position.
2. **Withdraw the exempt-surplus-land declaration**; redeclare as non-exempt surplus land; issue NOAs now to HCD-listed housing sponsors and relevant park/school entities; run the 60-day priority-negotiation window in good faith.
3. **Fix the affordability terms regardless of exemption strategy**: extend the covenant to at least 55 years (rental), and either (a) accept non-exempt status with the ~15% lower-income floor already technically met, or (b) increase genuinely lower-income units to the threshold needed to actually qualify for the mixed-income exemption (moderate-income units can stay as an additional, non-counted layer). Extending the covenant term alone materially strengthens the gift-clause defense independent of the exemption question.
4. **Redesign the local preference**: narrow to a displacement-based rationale, reduce the percentage or convert to a tie-breaker, add region-wide affirmative marketing, and commission a written AFFH assessment before re-adoption. Confirm compliance with any CTCAC local-preference cap if tax credits are involved.
5. **Adopt a comprehensive council resolution** with explicit, evidence-backed findings: public purpose; a gap/residual-value analysis supporting the $1-plus-in-kind exchange; SLA compliance path taken; and the AFFH analysis supporting the redesigned preference.
6. **Add enforceable recourse** to the DDA — completion guaranty/bond for the plaza, reversion rights for affordability-covenant breach — both as good practice and as gift-clause insurance.
7. **Engage HCD proactively** with a corrective action plan responding to the notice of violation, to avoid AG referral and the 50%-tier penalty.
8. **Engage the fair-housing organization before suit is filed** — a negotiated preference redesign is far cheaper than litigating a well-grounded disparate-impact claim, and it simultaneously improves the AFFH and gift-clause posture.
9. **Consider whether a validation action (Code Civ. # Memo: Vista Camino / Pacifica DDA — SLA, Gift Clause, and Fair Housing Exposure

**Bottom line up front:** (1) The SLA claim is a loser for the city as structured — the parcel does not fit any exempt-surplus category, the ENA-before-notice sequence was itself a violation, and even the deal's substantive terms (25-year covenants) independently violate § 54233. (3) The 50% five-year local preference is likely indefensible as designed in a 78%-white city in a 52%-white region, but it is the most fixable of the three. The restructuring path runs through the SLA cure, and fixing the SLA largely fixes the gift clause for free.

## 1. Surplus Land Act

### a. No exempt-surplus category fits

Under Gov. Code § 54221(f)(1), the exemption the city is presumably claiming is subd. (f)(1)(F) — the affordable-housing exemption. It has two prongs, and the project fails both:

- **Prong (i)** requires **100% of residential units restricted to low- or moderate-income households, with at least 75% restricted to lower-income households** (≤80% AMI, Health & Saf. Code § 50079.5), with covenants of **at least 55 years (rental) / 45 years (ownership)**, *and* disposition through an **open competitive bid to which all § 54222(a) entities are invited**. The project is 30% affordable, only 15% lower-income, with 25-year covenants, and there was no competitive process. Fails on every element.
- **Prong (ii)** (mixed-use) requires roughly **300+ units with at least 25% lower-income** and the same covenant terms. 120 units, 15% lower-income, 25 years. Fails.

Note the categorical point HCD is making in its NOV: **moderate-income units (80–120% AMI) never count toward "lower income" thresholds**, and **25-year covenants never satisfy the SLA anywhere in the statute** — the floor is 55/45 years throughout. No other category in § 54221(f)(1)(A)–(Q) (small parcels to contiguous owners, agency-to-agency transfers, exchanges, former rights-of-way, etc.) is plausible on these facts.

### b. Process that was owed

Because the land is (non-exempt) **surplus land**, the city owed:

1. **Declaration** of surplus status at a regular public meeting, supported by written findings, *before any action to dispose* (§ 54221(b)(1)). Under the AB 480 (2023) amendments, exempt-surplus declarations for most categories also require advance notice to HCD (roughly 30 days) with supporting findings — apparently skipped here.
2. **Notices of availability** to HCD, affordable-housing sponsors, park/rec agencies, and school districts (§ 54222) — *before* entering exclusive negotiations. HCD's SLA Guidelines are explicit that an ENA is an "action to dispose"; the ENA itself was a violation, not just the DDA.
3. **60 days** for notices of interest, then **at least 90 days of good-faith negotiation** with responding entities, with statutory priority to affordable-housing developers and, among competing proposals, to the deepest/greatest affordability (§§ 54222–54227).
4. **Pre-disposition HCD review**: submission of a description of negotiations to HCD, which has 30 days to flag violations, with a **60-day cure window** (§ 54230.5(b)).
5. Even after full compliance, a sale to a residential developer of 10+ units requires **≥15% lower-income units with recorded 55/45-year covenants** (§ 54233). The deal nominally hits 15% lower-income (18/120) but the 25-year term violates § 54233 *on its own* — meaning this deal was substantively illegal even in the counterfactual where the process was perfect.

### c. What the NOV and penalty mean

- **Penalty exposure:** For post-2020 dispositions, § 54230.5 imposes a penalty of **30% (first violation; 50% for subsequent)**. As amended by AB 480, the base is the **greater of the final sale price or fair market value** — the Legislature closed the exact $1-sale loophole this deal tried to use. Exposure ≈ **30% × $11.8M ≈ $3.54M**. Penalty funds must go to affordable housing (local housing trust fund or designated state funds) and be spent within roughly five years — so it's not "paying a fee to keep the deal"; it's a forced affordable-housing appropriation *on top of* whatever litigation does to the deal.
- **Timing is everything:** Penalty liability attaches to *disposing after HCD notice of violation*. **If escrow has not closed, the city can avoid the penalty entirely by not conveying** and using the 60-day cure window. If title has passed, penalty exposure is live, HCD can refer to the Attorney General, and beneficially interested parties (the taxpayer association, housing sponsors who never got notice) can sue in mandamus. The SLA doesn't expressly void completed conveyances, but courts can unwind via writ, and the parallel § 526a suit gives an independent unwinding vehicle.
- **Real-world signal:** This is the Angel Stadium fact pattern. HCD's NOV to Anaheim over its below-market stadium-land disposition produced a settlement earmarking ~$96M for affordable housing before the deal collapsed entirely. HCD enforces, the AG backs it, and NOVs are not opening bids to be lawyered around.

**Verdict: clear violation, both procedural and substantive. Probability of successfully defending the exemption claim: very low.**

## 2. Gift of Public Funds (Cal. Const. art. XVI, § 6)

### a. Doctrine

The conveyance of an $11.8M parcel for $1 is a transfer of a "thing of value" to a private corporation, so the question is the **public purpose doctrine**: an expenditure or conveyance is not a "gift" if it serves a public purpose, the determination of which belongs to the legislative body and is upheld unless "totally arbitrary." (*County of Alameda v. Janssen* (1940) 16 Cal.2d 276; *Scott v. State Bd. of Equalization* (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1597.) Affordable housing is a settled public purpose (*California Housing Finance Agency v. Elliott* (1976) 17 Cal.3d 575), and incidental private benefit does not defeat it. Courts do not require dollar-for-dollar consideration; redevelopment-era land write-downs for affordable housing were routine and routinely upheld.

### b. Honest valuation of the "consideration"

On deference alone, the city probably survives the pure gift-clause count: a 4–1 council vote, deed-restricted units, and a public plaza are enough public purpose for rational-basis-style review. But run the numbers the way plaintiff's expert will:

- 18 moderate-income units in most California markets carry **little or no rent gap** — near-zero subsidy value.
- 18 lower-income units for only **25 years** might carry an NPV subsidy of $150–250K/unit ≈ **$3–4.5M**.
- Plaza construction plus a maintenance obligation ≈ **$2–3M**.
- Total public consideration plausibly **$5–7M against $11.8M**, with the residual land value flowing to 84 market-rate units on free land.

That gap probably still survives the gift clause given deference — but it's exactly the record that supports a **CCP § 526a waste/illegal-expenditure claim**, where the taxpayer doesn't need to win the constitutional argument at all: *the conveyance violates the SLA, and an illegal disposition is enjoinable regardless of public purpose.* (Cf. *Harman v. City & County of San Francisco* (1972) 7 Cal.3d 150 — taxpayer challenge to city land sold for grossly inadequate consideration.) The gift clause is the headline of the complaint; § 526a plus the HCD NOV is the theory that wins it.

### c. Procedural armor the city skipped

- **Gov. Code §§ 52200–52203 (AB 562):** a below-FMV conveyance for economic development/housing requires a public hearing and a summary report stating cost, fair market value, highest-use value, the consideration received, and an explanation of the difference. Doing this — with a real appraisal of the in-kind benefits — is how you build a gift-clause-proof record. It apparently wasn't done.
- **Gov. Code § 65402** general-plan conformity report; standard checklist item.
- **Art. XXXIV** check: with ≤49% low-income units, private ownership, and no city development role, this is not a "low-rent housing project" (Health & Saf. Code § 37001), so no voter approval needed. One less problem.

**Verdict: the gift-clause count standing alone is defensible; the § 526a illegality count is not, because it borrows the SLA violation. Fix the SLA and quantify the consideration, and this exposure largely evaporates.**

## 3. The Local-Preference Covenant

### a. Disparate impact under the FHA and FEHA

A preference giving **50% of affordable units** to five-year residents/workers of a city that is **78% non-Hispanic white in a 52% region** is a textbook disparate-impact target:

- **FHA:** Under *Texas Dept. of Housing v. Inclusive Communities Project* (2015) and HUD's discriminatory-effects rule (2013 standard, restored 2023), plaintiff must show a policy causing a statistical disparity with "robust causality." Here causality is not attenuated: the policy *is* the demographic filter. The eligible regional applicant pool for low/mod units will be far more diverse than the five-year Vista Camino resident/worker pool; the preference mechanically reproduces the city's whiteness in half the affordable units.
- **FEHA:** Gov. Code § 12955.8(b) codifies a discriminatory-effects standard, § 12955.6 requires construction *no less protective* than the FHA, and the Civil Rights Council regulations adopt an effects test without the *Inclusive Communities* causality gloss. The state claim is, if anything, easier for plaintiffs.
- **AFFH:** Gov. Code § 8899.50 (AB 686) requires every public agency to administer housing programs so as to affirmatively further fair housing and to **"take no action materially inconsistent"** with that duty — enforceable in mandamus. A preference that entrenches an exclusionary demographic pattern in an already-exclusionary jurisdiction is close to the paradigm of "materially inconsistent." HCD's AFFH guidance specifically flags residency preferences in low-diversity jurisdictions. And note who enforces AFFH and reviews the housing element: **HCD — the same agency holding the SLA NOV.**
- **Durational element:** the five-year requirement adds a constitutional right-to-travel vulnerability (*Shapiro v. Thompson*; *Saenz v. Roe*; cf. *Thompson v. Mellon* (1973) 9 Cal.3d 96). Durational residency conditions on government-created benefits draw heightened scrutiny; the "or worked" prong and preference-vs.-eligibility framing soften but don't eliminate this.

The burden-shifting defense ("community stability," letting locals benefit from a local subsidy) is legally cognizable, but it fails at the **less-discriminatory-alternative** step because obvious alternatives exist (below). Compare the decade-long *Winfield* litigation over NYC's 50% community-preference policy: whatever its ultimate merits, it demonstrates that this design draws years of federal discovery into the jurisdiction's demographics and intent — and NYC's preference at least favored residents of *diverse* districts as an anti-displacement measure. Vista Camino's runs the opposite direction, closer to the exclusionary-suburb fact patterns (e.g., HUD's initial rejection of even San Francisco's neighborhood preference in a federally funded project).

### b. Can it be redesigned? Yes.

1. **Kill the five-year durational requirement** — it's the most legally toxic element and adds nothing defensible.
2. **Shrink the preference share** (e.g., ≤25% of affordable units) so it's a weighted lottery factor, not a set-aside.
3. **Retarget from residency to displacement risk**: preference for lower-income households displaced from or at risk of displacement within the jurisdiction, plus local workers. SB 649 (2022) expressly authorizes local anti-displacement tenant preferences structured to be consistent with fair-housing law — that's the legislatively blessed template.
4. **Region-wide affirmative marketing plan** as a condition of the DDA, with demographic monitoring and a sunset/re-evaluation clause.
5. **Do the disparate-impact analysis before adoption** (regional eligible-pool demographics vs. preference-pool demographics) and put it in the record — both as an FHA defense and as § 8899.50 compliance.
6. **Structurally: get it out of the deed.** A 25-year recorded covenant embedding a possibly illegal preference is a title problem you can't amend when the law or HUD/HCD guidance shifts. Put preferences in an amendable regulatory/leasing agreement.

**Verdict: as designed, more likely than not unlawful under FEHA/AFFH and seriously exposed under the FHA; fully salvageable via redesign.**

## 4. How the Three Exposures Interact

- **The SLA is the load-bearing violation.** It supplies the illegality element for the taxpayer's § 526a claim (mooting the city's gift-clause deference defense), it triggers the $3.54M penalty, and HCD's NOV is Exhibit A in every other case.
- **The SLA cure fixes the gift clause almost automatically.** SLA compliance means 55/45-year covenants, deeper or broader affordability, priority negotiation with housing sponsors, and (if the exemption path is taken) an open competitive bid. Each of those *increases the public consideration* and creates an arms-length record — exactly what the gift/waste defense needs. Add the § 52201 summary report quantifying the write-down, and the taxpayer suit is reduced to a deference argument the city wins.
- **The preference cuts against the cure.** Deepening affordability to satisfy the SLA puts *more* units under a discriminatory preference, enlarging the fair-housing exposure. And because HCD wears both hats (SLA enforcement and AFFH/housing-element oversight, including decertification leverage and downstream builder's-remedy consequences), defending the preference while negotiating the SLA cure is strategically incoherent. Concede the preference redesign as part of the HCD resolution.
- **Penalty vs. restructuring math:** $3.54M penalty + defense costs + unwinding risk vs. a restructured deal. If escrow hasn't closed, there is no serious argument for closing.

The panel converged tightly (range 80–84, σ 1.2), but four distinct disagreements survived mutual feedback. Panelists explicitly engaged each other's points and *declined to move* — so these are considered splits, not oversights.

---

## 1. What "substantially prevail" means (the largest persistent split)

**Crux, lower cluster (80, 81, 81):** The proposition's "held unlawful / substantially prevail" framing imports remedy-stage outcomes. If a court agrees with HCD's characterization but narrows relief to penalties-plus-prospective-compliance, credits a cure period, or blesses a negotiated resolution short of unwinding the DDA, the proposition might not resolve as "correct." One panelist stated this "remains a legitimate basis" for staying lower; another called it "a genuine ceiling on confidence rather than resolving it either way."

**Crux, upper cluster (82, 82, 82, 84):** The proposition's own parenthetical confines the question to exemption-qualification and process-compliance — a merits characterization. Remedy-softening doctrines (substantial compliance, estoppel, laches, cure) "go to scope of relief rather than the legal-characterization question the proposition actually asks" and are therefore orthogonal.

**Source of split:** Primarily a *proposition-interpretation* disagreement (how to read "substantially prevail"), layered over a *genuinely unsettled legal question* — how much daylight California courts allow between finding an SLA violation and ordering full invalidation.

**What would resolve it:** A precise operationalization of the proposition's resolution criteria; or, on the legal layer, authority establishing whether SLA violations post-AB 1486 support DDA invalidation versus §54230.5 penalties with prospective compliance, and whether substantial-compliance/cure defenses are cognizable at all.

---

## 2. NOA violation: freestanding second pillar vs. derivative consequence

**Crux, majority view (roughly 5 of 7):** The §54222 Notice-of-Availability failure is an *independently sufficient* violation — mechanically provable, undisputed on the facts — giving HCD a second path to prevail even if the exemption analysis proves closer than expected. One panelist emphasized the facts show "a total failure to notice rather than a documented but erroneous exemption finding."

**Crux, minority view (2 panelists, one of whom adopted this framing during the final round):** The NOA duty attaches only to non-exempt surplus land, so the notice failure is "the automatic consequence of the exemption claim failing, not a second freestanding violation." The case rests on "one strong, unified pillar," which one panelist expressly cited as a reason to cap confidence in the low-80s rather than push toward 85–90.

**Source of split:** A disagreement about *legal architecture* — statutory structure of §54221(f)/§54222 — not about outcome direction. All seven agree the process was owed and skipped. But it functions as a *risk-tolerance* driver: those who see two independent grounds price in more redundancy against error.

**What would resolve it:** A court's characterization of whether an NOA violation can be found and remedied independently of resolving the exemption question, or facts showing whether the city made any documented exemption finding before entering the ENA.

---

## 3. How much weight convergence-despite-divergent-recollections deserves

**Crux, upper end (84, and the movers from 80→82):** The fact that seven panelists reconstructed *incompatible* statutory thresholds yet all found the deal fails is "cross-validating evidence" — a robustness signal warranting upward movement.

**Crux, lower end (80):** That same convergence is discounted because "convergence among people relying on the same imperfect memory is weaker evidence than convergence among people independently verifying primary text" — a shared limitation cannot substitute for verification, which "caps how far I am willing to raise my estimate on that basis alone."

**Source of split:** Pure *epistemic risk tolerance* applied to a shared, acknowledged factual gap (all 7 self-flagged reliance on memory across the AB 1486 / AB 1255 / SB 6 / AB 2135 / AB 480 amendment cycles). No panelist disputes the gap exists; they disagree on how much the convergence pattern offsets it.

**What would resolve it:** The verified current text of Gov. Code §54221(f) — exact lower-income percentage, covenant-duration floor, and subsection location — including whether any later amendment created a mixed-income, transit-oriented, cure, or safe-harbor pathway that could accommodate a 30%-affordable / 25-year structure. Multiple panelists identified this as the single fact most capable of changing the analysis.

## 4. Minor residual variables (raised, not resolved)

- **Charter-city status:** One panelist originally raised the §54234 / statewide-concern point; two credited it as removing a defense and moved up partly on it; the panelist at 84 noted Vista Camino's charter status is *unstated in the facts* and kept it on the uncertainty list. Resolvable by a single fact (is the city charter or general-law) plus verification of the SLA's charter-city coverage.
- **Unrecalled alternative exemption:** All panelists note no plausible alternative §54221(f) category was identified, but the lower cluster preserves residual probability for an "obscure alternate exemption category" or grandfathering provision none of them recalled; the upper cluster treats the exhaustive-search failure as affirmative evidence. Resolvable only against the full current statutory text.

## 2. *Yamaha* cuts against deference here, not for it

The thesis invokes *Yamaha Corp. of America v. State Bd. of Equalization* (1998) 19 Cal.4th 1 for the proposition that HCD's reading is entitled to weight. **This citation should be read carefully before it is relied on, because *Yamaha*'s actual framework points toward less deference on these facts, not more.**

*Yamaha* holds that the weight due an agency's interpretation turns on factors including: (a) whether the agency has genuine technical/interstitial expertise on the specific point; (b) whether the interpretation is consistent and longstanding versus a position adopted for purposes of the immediate dispute; and (c) whether it was adopted through a process (formal rulemaking, contemporaneous guidance) that provides indicia of reliability, as opposed to a post hoc litigating position crafted to win a specific enforcement action. Applying those factors here:

- An NOV issued in the course of an active enforcement dispute against a specific city, on a specific transaction, is close to the paradigm of a "litigating position" *Yamaha* treats with skepticism, not the paradigm of a stable, contemporaneous interpretive rule.
- The thesis's own framing — that HCD Guidelines are APA-exempt (Gov. Code § 54233 or successor — **verify**) — is a double-edged fact. APA exemption means the guidance was never subjected to notice-and-comment vetting. Courts sometimes discount exactly this kind of informal guidance when a regulated party had no meaningful opportunity to test it before being penalized on the strength of it — an argument sounding in fair notice as much as in *Yamaha* deference proper.
- Ultimately, whether a given percentage/duration combination satisfies a statutory exemption is a **pure question of statutory construction**, which *Yamaha* itself identifies as the kind of question courts resolve independently rather than by deferring to the agency's preferred reading. 19 Cal.4th at 7–8 (courts retain ultimate responsibility for interpreting statutes) — **verify pinpoint**, but this is the standard understanding of *Yamaha*'s holding, and it is the correct rebuttal to an agency invoking its own authority as the reason its interpretation should win.

**Net effect:** the deference argument is, at best, a wash, and quite possibly a net negative for HCD once properly unpacked. This does not mean the city wins on the merits — it means one of the thesis's stated reasons for confidence should be discounted.

# SYNTHESIS — Surplus Land Act Proposition

**Operation selected: (a) RESOLVE.**

The apparent conflict between thesis and antithesis is not a genuine doctrinal fork requiring a coin-flip. It dissolves under a single interpretive move — reading the proposition through its own parenthetical — combined with three subsidiary distinctions that separate questions the two components were actually agreeing on from questions they were actually disputing. What remains after the dissolution is not irreducible disagreement but a short list of *confirmable facts* (city resolution language, ENA/DDA dates) that would pin down the residual uncertainty. That is a resolvable-pending-verification posture, not a crux.

---

## The move that does the work: read "held unlawful" through its own parenthetical

The proposition states: *"the disposition would be held unlawful under the Surplus Land Act... **(i.e., HCD's position that the parcel did not qualify as exempt surplus land and that the required process was violated would substantially prevail)**."*

The parenthetical is not ornamental — it *defines* what "held unlawful" means for purposes of this proposition. It equates "held unlawful" with **HCD's stated position substantially prevailing**, and HCD's stated position, per the facts, is: (1) no exemption applies, (2) the notice process was violated, (3) a 30% penalty is warranted. Nothing in the NOV as described claims entitlement to rescind the DDA or void the conveyance.

Once that's fixed, antithesis §1 — "the proposition smuggles in a remedy conclusion the statute doesn't clearly authorize" — is answering a question the proposition doesn't ask. The proposition is scoped to *liability and HCD's enforcement theory*, not to *unwind relief*. Thesis's own §6 already conceded the remedy point ("remedy scope is more contestable than liability"); antithesis's §1 develops that concession at length but directed it at the wrong target — it's a correct point about a claim nobody is actually making once the parenthetical is honored.

**This resolves the single largest apparent gap between the components.** Antithesis is right that rescission is uncertain. Thesis is right that liability substantially prevails. They don't conflict — they're answering different questions, and the proposition, correctly read, asks the one thesis answered.

---

## Applying the same move to the remaining disputed points

**1. Deference (*Yamaha*).** Both components treat this as a live fight. It mostly isn't, once you separate the two things HCD's exemption theory rests on. The 30%-vs-100% mix and the (likely) 25-year-vs-45/55-year covenant gap are not close statutory-construction questions on which reasonable readers could differ — they're numerical comparisons against a stated threshold. Deference doctrine, *Yamaha* included, exists to allocate interpretive weight where the statutory text is genuinely open-textured; it does nothing when the text says "100 percent" and the deal delivers "30 percent." So: **antithesis's *Yamaha* critique is correct as a general proposition but immaterial to the numeric core of the case.** It becomes live only on the secondary, genuinely interpretive questions — "sole purpose" given the plaza, whether any blended-threshold exemption exists, and how any transition/savings clause in AB 1486/SB 6 should be read. On *those* questions, antithesis's point that a litigating-position NOV gets weaker *Yamaha* weight than a stable, contemporaneous rule is the better-supported view, and the reviewing attorney should not lean on agency deference to close those gaps — they need to be closed by text and dates instead.

**2. "Two independent grounds" vs. "parasitic" process violation.** This is a genuine distinction, not a disagreement, once you separate *doctrinal* independence from *evidentiary* independence:
- **Doctrinally**, antithesis is correct: the NOA obligation is triggered *by* the exemption determination failing, so there is one legal question (exempt or not) with two labeled consequences, not two freestanding failures.
- **Evidentially/remedially**, thesis's point survives intact: the fact that the city ran an ENA with a single private developer *before* any notice process, and never attempted to cure, is independent evidence bearing on penalty severity and on rebutting a "we made a good-faith, colorable exemption call" defense. Predetermination evidence doesn't need its own statutory hook to matter — it matters for how a court or HCD treats the *one* violation once found.

Net: relabel from "two independent grounds" to "one liability determination, with the sequencing serving as aggravating evidence relevant to remedy and good-faith defenses." Both components' substantive content survives; only the "independent" framing needs correction.

**3. Cure-architecture / likelihood of ever reaching a judicial holding.** Antithesis's §5 argument — that HCD enforcement typically produces negotiated cures, not judgments, so the deal may never be tested — is a good client-counseling point and should stay in the memo. But it doesn't bear on the *proposition's truth value*, because the proposition is explicitly conditional: **"if challenged as structured."** That clause stipulates away the empirical question of whether a challenge actually proceeds to adjudication; it asks what happens *given* a challenge on the current facts. Antithesis's point is real and important for a "what should the city do" recommendation, but it's an answer to "will this get litigated," not to "would HCD's position substantially prevail if it is." Filed under practical-counseling caveats, not doctrinal rebuttal.

**4. Standing/vehicle problems (mandamus, validation actions, § 54234).** Same treatment as #3: relevant to *who* can bring the claim and *whether* it gets to a decisionmaker, not to whether HCD's position is correct once before one. Worth flagging for the taxpayer-association and fair-housing-org suits (separate pipeline components handle those), but it doesn't weaken HCD's own enforcement theory, which is the subject of this proposition.

---

## What's left: confirmable facts, not an irreducible crux

After the above, both components substantially agree on the merits question the proposition actually poses. What's left is not a disagreement about law but two unresolved *facts*, either of which could genuinely flip the outcome and neither of which either component could confirm from the record given:

1. **Which exemption category the city's resolution actually invoked**, and whether any post-2019 amendment created a blended/lower-percentage threshold neither component is aware of. (Pull the resolution; pull current § 54221(f)(1) in full.)
2. **The dates of ENA and DDA approval against AB 1486 (2020 operative) and SB 6 (2023) effective dates**, and whether either enactment contains a savings/transition clause for agencies already in negotiation. If the pre-amendment framework governs, the specific percentage/duration thresholds thesis relies on may not be the correct legal standard, even though a violation of *some* version of the Act likely still exists given a 30%/25-year structure is short of essentially every version of this exemption on record.

These aren't "what a higher court would need to decide" — they're facts sitting in a file, confirmable before any litigation begins. That's what makes this a **resolution**, not an irreducible tension: the framework tells the reviewing attorney exactly what to pull, and the answer doesn't hinge on an unresolved legal question but on documents that exist right now.

---

## Bottom line

**HCD's stated position — no exemption applies, process was violated, penalty exposure is real — substantially prevails on the liability question the proposition actually asks, subject to confirming (1) which exemption was invoked and (2) that current-law thresholds (not a pre-amendment version) govern this transaction.** Rescission/unwinding the DDA is a separate, genuinely more uncertain question that the proposition's own parenthetical excludes; don't let a liability win get oversold into a rescission prediction. Whether a judgment is ever actually entered (versus a negotiated cure) is a practical prediction outside the proposition's "if challenged" stipulation and belongs in client counseling, not in the answer to this question.

### The steelman

## I. The Surplus Land Act violation is foundational — it voids the exemption, requires a full restart, and triggers the mandatory penalty in full

The Surplus Land Act (Gov. Code §§ 54220–54234, substantially strengthened by AB 1486 (2019)) is built on a deliberately sequenced priority scheme: before a local agency may negotiate the disposal of land it no longer needs with anyone — let alone a private, for-profit developer — it must determine whether the parcel is "surplus land" under Government Code section 54221, and if so, issue a Notice of Availability under section 54222 giving statutorily enumerated priority entities (affordable housing sponsors, housing authorities, school districts, park and open-space agencies, community land trusts) the first opportunity to negotiate, followed by a mandatory good-faith negotiating window, before the agency ever looks to the private market. That sequence is not a formality. It exists so that scarce publicly owned land cannot be quietly captured by a private developer before the entities the Legislature identified as deserving first access ever learn the parcel was available. Vista Camino skipped the sequence entirely — no notice went to a single housing sponsor, park agency, or school district. It went straight to an ENA with Pacifica.

The only way that omission is lawful is if the parcel qualifies as "exempt surplus land" under section 54221(f) — a narrow carve-out for dispositions meeting specific income-mix and durational-covenant requirements. HCD says it does not, and it is right on the City's own facts. The exemption is not satisfied by attaching "affordable" to a third of the units. It requires deep, durable affordability restricted to lower-income households — not blended with moderate-income units, which serve households up to 120% of area median income under Health & Safety Code section 50093 and are not the population the exemption is calibrated around — for a term consistent with the floor California and federal law treat as genuine long-term affordability in every comparable subsidized-housing context: 55 years for rental restrictions under the density bonus law (Gov. Code § 65915(c)(1)) and the federal low-income housing tax credit program (26 U.S.C. § 42(h)(6)). Half of the City's "affordable" units — 18 of 36 — are moderate-income, meaning only 15% of the development, not 30%, serves the population the exemption is designed around; and the 25-year covenant the City accepted is less than half the recognized floor, expiring while the building is still in the first half of its useful life and handing Pacifica an unencumbered, fully appreciated $11.8M asset for the rest. HCD's notice targets exactly these two defects because they are exactly the two that matter.

Because the exemption fails, the parcel was surplus land from the outset, and the NOA obligation attached before the ENA was ever signed. That is not a curable paperwork defect fixable by amending the DDA's covenant term while keeping Pacifica as the counterparty. The NOA/negotiation sequence exists so priority entities can compete for the land *before* the agency commits to a private buyer; sending notices now, after the City has already selected, negotiated with, and contracted with a private developer, does not replicate that process — it ratifies the sequencing violation after the fact and forecloses the very competition the statute protects. The only disposition consistent with the Act is to unwind the DDA and restart at the surplus-land declaration, with no assurance Pacifica remains the developer at the end of it — because that is the point.

Nor is the exposure a fine to be negotiated down. Section 54230.5's enforcement provisions exist because the pre-2019 Act had no teeth and cities ignored it; the reform deliberately made noncompliance expensive so agencies could not treat the Act as a risk to be priced. With the "disposition price" at $1, the operative penalty base is fair market value: 30% of $11.8M, on top of the underlying transaction's invalidity. Treating that figure as a starting point for a negotiated settlement with HCD reintroduces the discretionary softness the 2019 reform was enacted to eliminate. The statute reads as mandatory once a violation is found; the correct posture is that the penalty is owed, not that it is a bargaining chip.

## II. The $1 conveyance is an actual, unconstitutional gift of public funds — the public-purpose doctrine does not reach this transaction

Article XVI, section 6 prohibits gifts of public money or things of value except for specified public purposes. Courts have long allowed a public purpose to validate a transfer that would otherwise look like a gift, but that doctrine has never meant any transfer labeled "affordable housing" escapes scrutiny regardless of magnitude or substance. A gift, as courts have framed it since *County of Alameda v. Janssen* (1940) 16 Cal.2d 276, is a transfer without consideration reasonably equivalent to what is given up; the public-purpose doctrine supplies a reason the public *may* spend on private parties, it does not relieve the agency of actually receiving value back, and gross disproportion remains judicially testable.

This disproportion is not marginal — an $11.8M appraised asset for $1, a spread of roughly 11.8 million to one, to a private, for-profit LLC that holds fee title forever. What the City receives back is not equivalent value; it is a bundle of promises with structural defects that make them worth a fraction of face value. Thirty-six units are restricted for 25 years on an asset the developer owns and profits from indefinitely; when the covenants expire, Pacifica converts to market rate and keeps 100% of an asset the public paid for. The plaza is "built and maintained by the developer" — the City owns no interest in it and has no self-executing mechanism assuring its upkeep decades hence beyond whatever enforcement it is willing to pursue against a private landlord. Nothing the City receives sits on the public balance sheet; everything it gives up did. That is the paradigm case gift-clause doctrine exists to catch: a transaction described in public-benefit language that functions, in substance, as a transfer of public wealth into private equity.

This is not *Housing Authority of City of Los Angeles v. Dockweiler* (1939) 14 Cal.2d 437, which upheld public investment in low-rent housing where the public itself owned and controlled the asset — not a fee-simple conveyance to a private for-profit developer with no reversion and no continuing public stake. Nor is it *California Housing Finance Agency v. Elliott* (1976) 17 Cal.3d 575, which upheld a financing program administered directly by a public agency, not a below-cost land grant to a third-party LLC. Every case upholding a subsidy to a private developer against a gift-clause challenge involves the public retaining a comparably durable interest — a ground lease preserving title, a covenant term matched to the economic life of the subsidy, a reversion, or a documented, appraisal-based valuation of the subsidy actually being provided. Vista Camino did none of that; it did not commission an appraisal of what the affordability restriction was worth and price the conveyance at fair market value minus that documented figure — it picked $1 and pointed at a term sheet. If the City genuinely wanted to subsidize affordable housing with this land, the doctrinally sound tools existed: retain the fee via ground lease, extend the covenant to the 55-year/45-year floor, or build in shared appreciation so the public recaptures value on resale or decontrol. None of that happened, and the absence of any of it is exactly why this transaction looks — and functions — as a gift rather than a documented public-purpose subsidy.

## III. The five-year residency preference independently violates fair-housing law and the AFFH duty, and cannot be saved by redesign short of elimination or de-linking from local tenure

The preference is not a compliance risk to be managed with careful drafting; it is unlawful on the facts as given, for the plainest possible reason: durational residency or employment in Vista Camino is, on the City's own demographics, a proxy for race. A jurisdiction that is 78% non-Hispanic white inside a metro region that is 52% non-Hispanic white does not have a demographically neutral population of "people who have lived or worked here five years." Reserving half the affordable units for that population predictably and substantially skews the beneficiary pool toward the group already overrepresented locally and away from the region's protected-class households — the very population regional affordability and AFFH obligations exist to reach.

That satisfies the disparate-impact framework of *Texas Dept. of Housing & Cmty. Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project, Inc.* (2015) 576 U.S. 519 under the FHA and FEHA (Gov. Code § 12955). ICP requires robust causality and warns against bare statistics untethered to mechanism — but here there is no daylight between statistic and mechanism: the eligibility criterion *is* existing local presence, in a jurisdiction whose local presence is itself the demographic legacy of exclusionary suburban land-use patterns. This is structurally the design already confronted in *United States v. City of Parma, Ohio* (6th Cir. 1980) 661 F.2d 562, which invalidated a municipal residency-type preference in a homogeneous suburb bordering a far more diverse metro area precisely because it preserved the suburb's existing composition while excluding the regional population the housing was meant to serve.

Under ICP's burden-shifting structure, the City will offer the justifications every jurisdiction offers — protecting long-term residents from displacement, rewarding local workers, community stability. These do not survive ICP's second step, because equally effective, non-discriminatory alternatives plainly exist: a preference tied to documented displacement from this specific redevelopment site (a right-to-return preference recognized in HUD/AFFH guidance as legitimate precisely because it responds to a discrete, project-caused harm, not general municipal membership), or an income/workforce sub-preference untethered to durational residency, would serve any genuine anti-displacement interest without importing a criterion that operates as a proxy for existing local racial composition. Where a less discriminatory alternative serves the interest equally well, ICP does not let the more discriminatory policy stand because its stated purpose sounds benign.

This is also an independent breach of the City's affirmative obligations, not merely litigation exposure. AB 686 codified a duty on public agencies to take meaningful action to overcome patterns of segregation and foster inclusion in administering housing programs (Gov. Code § 8899.50), running alongside the federal AFFH framework (24 C.F.R. § 5.150 et seq.). Disposing of a significant public asset specifically to create affordable housing, then designing the allocation mechanism so it is expected, on the City's own demographics, to reproduce the City's existing overrepresentation of its dominant group, is not a neutral design choice compatible with that duty — it is evidence the duty was never considered.

Redesign around the edges does not fix this. Shortening five years to two, or reducing the preference share from 50% to 25%, dilutes the magnitude without removing what makes the mechanism unlawful: it still sorts eligibility by pre-existing local presence in a jurisdiction whose local presence is demographically unrepresentative of the region the housing serves. A smaller dose of the same mechanism is still the same mechanism; nothing in ICP or *Parma* treats partial dilution as a defense once the causal link between criterion and disparity is established. The preference must either be eliminated in favor of a regionally open lottery, or replaced with a criterion that does not correlate with existing municipal residency at all — such as a preference limited to households actually displaced by this development. Anything still keyed to how long someone has already lived or worked in Vista Camino carries the same defect forward at smaller scale.

## IV. Why these three failures are one failure, and what compliance actually requires

These are not three unrelated risks the City happens to face at once; they are three symptoms of a single decision to treat a public asset and a public process as an internal matter to be worked out privately with one chosen developer, rather than as a resource subject to statutory sequencing, constitutional value-for-value discipline, and affirmative regional-equity obligations that all exist to prevent exactly this kind of insider disposition. Fixing one axis does not cure the others. Extending the covenant to 55 years and deepening the income mix cures nothing if the NOA process is never run and nothing close to value is ever charged. Charging fair value or restructuring as a ground lease cures nothing if the beneficiary-selection mechanism still locks out the regional population the subsidy exists to serve. Dropping the preference cures nothing if the exemption claim and the $1 price stand.

None of this reflects hostility to using public land aggressively to subsidize affordable housing, or indifference to the two years the City has already invested. *Dockweiler* and decades of California public-housing practice show public land can be used this way. The failure here was never the goal — it was the shortcut process, the price with no documented basis, and an allocation mechanism that undercuts the regional-access rationale the whole project depends on. A deal built to withstand an HCD compliance review, a gift-clause challenge, and a fair-housing complaint is a durable deal; a deal that depends on nobody scrutinizing the exemption claim, the appraisal gap, or the preference covenant is not affordable-housing policy — it is a liability the City was already carrying, which the taxpayer suit and HCD's notice have simply made visible. Sunk negotiating time is not consideration for continuing an unlawful process, and letting an advanced negotiation immunize noncompliance would tell every other city that speed to signature beats compliance — precisely the incentive the 2019 Surplus Land Act reforms and fair-housing law exist to eliminate.

The disposition that survives scrutiny on all three axes is the same disposition: restart at the surplus-land declaration and run the actual NOA/negotiation process open to every statutory priority category before recommitting to any developer; price the conveyance to real, appraisal-documented consideration — a ground lease preserving public title, a covenant term at the 55-year/45-year floor with a genuinely deep income mix, a recapture or shared-appreciation mechanism, or some combination an appraiser can show offsets the $11.8M gap — rather than $1 defended by unquantified "in-kind" benefits; and replace the five-year residency preference with either no local preference or one tied to documented displacement from this project, opening the units to the full regional applicant pool AFFH exists to protect. Anything short of that leaves the City exposed on all three fronts simultaneously, because all three exposures trace back to the same original choice: treating a scarce public asset as available for a private insider deal rather than as a resource state law requires be tested against public competition, real consideration, and regional fair access before it is given away.

## 2. Cost/benefit distribution across candidate resolutions

- **Negotiated cure with HCD (add units, extend covenant term, ratify notice retroactively)** — costs fall mostly on Pacifica (reduced market-rate revenue, longer restriction) and secondarily the city (reduced future asset flexibility). - **City litigates against HCD** — costs are litigation fees borne by city taxpayers (the same class the taxpayer association purports to protect), delay, and risk of an adverse published opinion binding *other* cities — an outcome HCD, backed by AG resources, can absorb far better than a mid-size city, making this a poor risk-adjusted bet for the city despite short-term political appeal to the 4-member majority.
- **Taxpayer suit succeeds outright** — a blunt instrument: it can only void the transaction, not redesign it into more affordable units. - **Fair-housing challenge to the preference** — most likely resolved by negotiated redesign (capped/weighted preference rather than absolute 50% priority) rather than full disparate-impact litigation, because the org's leverage (press, HUD/CRD administrative complaint option) generates settlement value without the evidentiary cost of proving impact.

**One-shotters** (bear disproportionate cost per unit of engagement): the taxpayer association (likely ad hoc, no damages recovery, high per-suit cost), individual council members (politically one-shot on election-cycle time horizons even though the city institution is a repeat player — this creates a horizon mismatch: the people who approved the deal answer to a 2-4 year electoral clock, not the multi-year regulatory relationship the city as an institution has with HCD), excluded housing sponsors (never entered the game at all), and the 36 future tenant households (total absence of standing or voice).

This is **not** one question with six inputs; it's three legally distinct exposures (statutory/SLA, constitutional/gift-clause, civil-rights/disparate-impact) that combine **disjunctively** at the top level — the City loses something if Track A **or** Track B **or** Track C lands — but each track has its own internal logic, and the tracks are not all independent of each other.

**Track A — SLA/HCD (Q1 → Q2 → Q3): a conjunctive chain, sequential/conditional.**
