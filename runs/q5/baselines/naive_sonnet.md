## 1. Surplus Land Act (Gov. Code §§ 54220–54234)

### 1.1 What process was owed

Before entering into an ENA or any disposition negotiation, a local agency must, at an open public meeting, make a written finding classifying the property as either:

- **"Exempt surplus land"** (one of the enumerated categories in § 54221(f)(1)) — if so classified, the notice/priority-negotiation machinery below doesn't apply, but the agency must still describe and document the exemption basis, and HCD can (and here did) dispute it; or
- **"Surplus land"** (non-exempt) — which triggers:
  1. A **Notice of Availability (NOA)** to every housing sponsor on HCD's registry and to specified local/park/school entities *before* the agency may negotiate a disposition;
  2. A **60-day priority negotiating window** during which any qualifying entity that responds — affordable-housing developers first, then park/open-space and school entities — gets exclusive good-faith negotiation rights ahead of a private market-rate buyer;
  3. If the land is ultimately sold for housing to a non-priority buyer, a **statutory floor** requiring not less than roughly 15% of units be affordable to *lower-income* households under an enforceable, recorded restriction;
  4. Reporting of the disposition terms to HCD, which has review and objection authority.

The city did none of the notice/priority-negotiation steps. It went straight to an ENA with Pacifica on the strength of a self-declared exemption. That means the procedural defect exists **independent of** whatever the substantive exemption analysis shows: even a perfectly-designed affordable housing deal entered without an NOA and 60-day window is a violation, because other priority entities (housing sponsors, the school district, a parks agency interested in that half-acre plaza site) never got the statutory chance to compete for the parcel.

### 1.2 Was any exemption actually available?

No — on the facts as given, for two independent reasons, and HCD's notice of violation is targeting exactly these two:

**(a) Income mix.** The affordable-housing exemption categories in the Act are keyed to units affordable to *lower-income* households (Health & Safety Code §§ 50079.5/50105 definitions), not "low or moderate" as a blended category. Moderate-income (up to ~120% AMI) units **do not count** toward the exemption threshold. Here, only 18 of 120 units — **15%**, not the advertised 30% — are true lower-income units. That falls short of whatever the applicable mixed-income exemption threshold is (the statute, post-AB 1486/AB 480, requires a meaningfully higher lower-income percentage, commonly cited around 25%, for that pathway to apply — verify the current figure against the codified text, as this provision has been amended repeatedly). Nor does the project qualify for the more generous **100%-affordable exemption**, since only 15% of units are lower-income restricted. The city's "30% affordable" framing is doing real rhetorical work to disguise a 15% lower-income project — precisely the kind of characterization HCD's enforcement program was built to catch.

**(b) Covenant term.** Every affordability-based exemption category requires a recorded deed restriction of **not less than 55 years for rental housing** (45 years for ownership). A 25-year covenant is independently, and unambiguously, fatal — it isn't a close call. Even if the income mix were fixed, a 25-year term alone forecloses exemption eligibility.

**Conclusion:** the parcel was not eligible for exempt surplus land status under any affordability-based category. HCD's position is legally sound on these facts.

### 1.3 Consequences

- **Penalty exposure.** Civil penalties under the Act run 30% of the greater of the disposition price or fair market value for a first violation, 50% for subsequent/continuing violations. Sale price here is $1, so the base is FMV: **30% × $11.8M ≈ $3.54M**. If the city proceeds without curing after HCD's notice, the 50% tier becomes a live risk (~$5.9M).
- **Voidability / injunctive exposure.** The Act creates a private right of action allowing an interested person (and separately, HCD via referral to the Attorney General) to seek to enjoin, or void, a disposition made in violation of the Act, in addition to compelling compliance. Combined with the taxpayer suit already filed, this is a real risk to the transaction's finality, not just a fine.
- **Practical posture with HCD.** HCD notice-of-violation letters typically open a negotiation for a corrective action plan before AG referral. That's the leverage point — and the reason curing quickly matters more than litigating the exemption question.

*(Caveat: subsection lettering and exact percentage/threshold figures in § 54221(f) have been amended several times — AB 1486 (2019), AB 1255/SB 6 (2019), AB 2135 (2022), AB 480 (2023) — confirm against current codified text before finalizing any filing. The substantive conclusions above — lower-income-only counting, 55/45-year minimums, the ~15% floor, and the 30%/50% penalty structure — are well-settled features of the Act and are not in serious doubt.)*

## 2. Gift of public funds (Cal. Const. art. XVI, § 6)

### 2.1 The doctrine, correctly framed

Article XVI, § 6's prohibition on gifts of public funds is analyzed under a **highly deferential** two-part inquiry, not ordinary contract-law "adequacy of consideration":

1. **Public purpose test** (*County of Alameda v. Carleson* (1971) 5 Cal.3d 730): if any legitimate public purpose can fairly be conceived for the expenditure, courts will not second-guess the legislative body's judgment about value — "the courts have no power to interfere" absent a showing the purpose is a sham.
2. **Consideration** matters mainly as *evidence*: gross disproportion between value given and value received can suggest the stated public purpose is pretextual, but courts do not require dollar-for-dollar equivalence. Non-cash, legally enforceable consideration — recorded covenants running with the land, construction and maintenance obligations — counts as real consideration (compare the pension-as-consideration reasoning in *County of Alameda v. Janssen* (1940) 16 Cal.2d 276; subsidized-housing-as-public-purpose reasoning in *California Housing Finance Agency v. Patitucci* (1978) 22 Cal.3d 171).

**Direct answer to the question posed:** you don't need both prongs independently satisfied — the public-purpose doctrine, standing alone, is normally sufficient to defeat a gift-clause claim, *provided* the disproportion isn't so extreme it reveals the "public purpose" as pretext. $1 plus enforceable in-kind obligations (affordable units, plaza construction and maintenance) is very likely to be treated as adequate consideration under this framework — this is the same structural logic that has long sustained redevelopment-era land write-downs to private developers in exchange for public benefits.

### 2.2 Why this deal is more exposed than the doctrine's usual comfort level

The vulnerability here isn't the doctrine — it's the facts feeding into it. HCD has effectively found, as a matter of state housing law, that the "affordable housing" benefit the city is citing as its public purpose is smaller and shorter-lived than advertised (15% true lower-income, 25-year term, not 30%/permanent-equivalent). A taxpayer plaintiff will argue: *the very benefit the city claims justifies an $11.8M-to-$1 spread doesn't even satisfy the state's own statutory floor for what counts as adequate affordable-housing consideration for surplus public land* — which is a much more concrete, judicially cognizable argument than a generic "$11.8M for $1 smells wrong" claim. The SLA finding doesn't automatically become a gift-clause violation, but it substantially strengthens the plaintiff's "sham public purpose" narrative and weakens the city's evidentiary record.

Conversely, curing the SLA problems (raising true lower-income percentage, extending the covenant to the 55-year minimum) directly strengthens the gift-clause defense — it makes the in-kind consideration larger, more durable, and independently validated by the state housing regulator.

### 2.3 What actually protects the city here

- **Contemporaneous, specific findings**: a council resolution making explicit findings on (a) the public purpose served (remediation of an underused corp yard, RHNA/housing-element production, publicly accessible open space), and (b) why the exchange is reasonably related to value given — ideally supported by an appraiser's **gap/residual-land-value analysis** quantifying the developer's forgone market rent over the covenant term plus plaza construction/maintenance cost. This doesn't need to equal $11.8M; it needs to show the city reasoned it through rather than waved it through.
- **Enforceable recourse**: recorded covenants pre-closing, completion guaranties/bonds for the plaza, and reversion rights if affordability or maintenance obligations lapse. Courts treat retained enforceable recourse as evidence the "consideration" is real, not illusory.
- **Procedural regularity**: SLA compliance (or a documented cure) is not a legal safe harbor for gift-clause purposes, but it is powerful evidentiary support for "this was a regular, deliberated public-benefit exchange," which is exactly what defeats a "sham" argument.

**Bottom line:** the gift-clause claim, litigated today on these facts, is more likely than not to survive a motion but is not a slam-dunk win for the city given the SLA overlay; after the SLA/preference cures recommended below, it becomes a comfortable win.

## 3. The five-year local-preference covenant — FHA/FEHA disparate impact and AFFH

### 3.1 Framework

- **FHA**: disparate-impact liability for facially neutral housing policies is settled federal law (*Texas Dept. of Housing & Community Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project* (2015) 576 U.S. 519), using a burden-shifting test: (1) plaintiff shows a robust statistical/causal disparity from the challenged policy; (2) defendant shows the policy is necessary to a substantial, legitimate, nondiscriminatory interest; (3) plaintiff may still win by showing an equally effective, less discriminatory alternative exists.
- **FEHA**: Gov. Code § 12955.8 codifies the same disparate-impact framework for California housing discrimination claims.
- **AB 686 / AFFH duty**: Gov. Code § 8899.50 imposes an *affirmative* duty on every public agency to administer housing-related activities — expressly including disposition of publicly owned land for housing — in a manner that affirmatively furthers fair housing. This is broader and more proactive than disparate-impact avoidance; it's a design obligation, not just a litigation defense, and it gives HCD an independent hook (on top of the SLA violation) to scrutinize this disposition.

### 3.2 Application

The 78%-white-city/52%-metro-region gap is a real signal, but plaintiffs will need **applicant-pool-level data** (not just general population figures) to satisfy *Inclusive Communities*' "robust causality" requirement — general demographics alone are suggestive, not sufficient, though they're enough to make this a credible threatened suit rather than a nuisance claim. The design features that make this preference especially exposed:

- It reaches **50% of the affordable units** — a large carve-out, not a modest tie-breaker;
- The qualifying criterion is a **generic durational residency-or-employment test**, unconnected to any actual displacement caused by this project or the corp-yard's prior use;
- It operates in a jurisdiction whose demographics already diverge sharply from the region it draws applicants from.

HUD and fair-housing litigation practice have long treated **broad, undifferentiated local/neighborhood preferences** in racially disparate jurisdictions as high-risk (this is the same fact pattern that has generated real-world HUD scrutiny of neighborhood-preference lotteries in segregated markets). The city's likely defenses — jobs-housing balance, community stability, political support for the project — are legitimate interests in the abstract, but a **generic 5-year test with no displacement nexus** is a weak fit for the "necessary to achieve" step, and is vulnerable at the "less discriminatory alternative" step because far more narrowly tailored designs exist (below).

### 3.3 Can it be saved by redesign? Yes — but not left as-is

Redesign options, roughly in order of legal defensibility:

1. **Convert to a true anti-displacement / right-to-return preference** — limited to households or individuals actually displaced by this project or the corp yard's operations. This is the best-established, most defensible model.
2. **Reduce the share and convert from an absolute set-aside to a tie-breaker** applied only among otherwise equally qualified applicants, rather than a screen that excludes non-local applicants outright.
3. **Reconsider the geographic/eligibility definition** (e.g., tie to specific local employers/essential-worker categories rather than generic residency) — but test any redesign against real applicant-pool data before adopting it; don't assume it fixes the problem.
4. **Add affirmative, region-wide marketing obligations** targeted at protected-class populations to counteract the pool-narrowing effect of any preference retained.
5. **Commission a written AFFH/disparate-impact assessment before re-adoption** — this both satisfies the AB 686 obligation directly and builds the administrative record that will matter if litigation follows.
6. **Check program-specific caps.** If the project uses 4%/9% LIHTC (likely, given the affordability structure), CTCAC regulations independently constrain local preferences — typically capping them well below 50% and requiring a displacement or comparable nexus. That cap applies regardless of how the FHA/FEHA analysis comes out, and the current 50% figure is likely non-compliant with those program rules on its own terms if tax credits are in the capital stack.

**Bottom line:** the preference as drafted is a genuine, well-founded exposure — not a nuisance claim — but it is readily curable through narrowing and an AFFH-grounded redesign, at comparatively low cost, before litigation is filed.

## 4. How the three exposures interact

These are not three independent risk silos — they reinforce each other in both directions:

- **SLA → Gift Clause:** HCD's finding that the "affordable housing" benefit doesn't meet the state's own floor (income mix, covenant term) directly undercuts the city's public-purpose/consideration narrative for the taxpayer suit. Curing the SLA defect enlarges and validates the in-kind consideration, which is the single most useful thing the city can do for its gift-clause defense.
- **Preference → SLA and Gift Clause:** A discriminatory design feature in the disposition is an aggravating fact HCD can cite (AFFH duties attach to land-disposition decisions specifically), and it undermines the "legitimate, unqualified public purpose" characterization a court needs to reject the gift-clause claim. It's hard to argue the deal serves an unimpeachable public purpose while defending a feature that predictably produces a whiter beneficiary pool than a regionally open process would.
- **Practical sequencing risk:** if the SLA violation leads to voiding or unwinding the disposition, the gift-clause and fair-housing claims may become moot for *this* transaction — but the city then faces developer breach exposure under the DDA (if closing conditions didn't anticipate this), the 30%/50% penalty, and the reputational/political cost of restarting. The far better path is curing in place rather than litigating the exemption question or waiting for a court to unwind the deal.

## 5. Recommended restructuring

1. **Do not close (or, if already recorded, pause performance and open a cooperative dialogue with HCD)** — a corrective-action posture, not a defend-the-exemption posture, is the right frame given the strength of HCD's position.
2. **Withdraw the exempt-surplus-land declaration**; redeclare as non-exempt surplus land; issue NOAs now to HCD-listed housing sponsors and relevant park/school entities; run the 60-day priority-negotiation window in good faith.
3. **Fix the affordability terms regardless of exemption strategy**: extend the covenant to at least 55 years (rental), and either (a) accept non-exempt status with the ~15% lower-income floor already technically met, or (b) increase genuinely lower-income units to the threshold needed to actually qualify for the mixed-income exemption (moderate-income units can stay as an additional, non-counted layer). Extending the covenant term alone materially strengthens the gift-clause defense independent of the exemption question.
4. **Redesign the local preference**: narrow to a displacement-based rationale, reduce the percentage or convert to a tie-breaker, add region-wide affirmative marketing, and commission a written AFFH assessment before re-adoption. Confirm compliance with any CTCAC local-preference cap if tax credits are involved.
5. **Adopt a comprehensive council resolution** with explicit, evidence-backed findings: public purpose; a gap/residual-value analysis supporting the $1-plus-in-kind exchange; SLA compliance path taken; and the AFFH analysis supporting the redesigned preference.
6. **Add enforceable recourse** to the DDA — completion guaranty/bond for the plaza, reversion rights for affordability-covenant breach — both as good practice and as gift-clause insurance.
7. **Engage HCD proactively** with a corrective action plan responding to the notice of violation, to avoid AG referral and the 50%-tier penalty.
8. **Engage the fair-housing organization before suit is filed** — a negotiated preference redesign is far cheaper than litigating a well-grounded disparate-impact claim, and it simultaneously improves the AFFH and gift-clause posture.
9. **Consider whether a validation action (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 860–870) is available** for this category of disposition once the deal is restructured, to obtain prompt judicial confirmation and cut off follow-on taxpayer challenges via the statute's short limitations period — worth confirming eligibility, not a certainty here.
